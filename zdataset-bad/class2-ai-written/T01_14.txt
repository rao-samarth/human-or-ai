The fluorescent lights of the town hall hummed, casting a pale glow on the rolled blueprints in Mark’s lap. This was it. The final approval. Then Elias Henderson shuffled to the podium, his back stooped from a lifetime of bending over engines. His voice, thin but clear, spoke of the small repair shop his father had opened, the one standing right in the middle of Mark’s proposed parking lot. Mark’s fingers tightened on the smooth paper. He could almost feel the cool glass and steel of the future he’d designed, a legacy for his own kids, the project that would finally put him on the map. But all he could see was Mr. Henderson’s worn-out work coat and the way his hands trembled on the wood. The mayor cleared his throat, looking directly at him. It was just business, a simple matter of progress. He repeated the words to himself, a silent chant that suddenly offered no comfort at all.
