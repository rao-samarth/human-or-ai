The train swayed gently, blurring the green countryside into long, soft strokes. On the phone screen, his brother’s message was a stark block of text. “Just tell them the basement flooded a little. That’s technically true.” It was true. A pipe had leaked. But it wasn't the whole story, not even the important part. It didn’t mention the gambling debt that the “flood damage” money was actually for. He could just forward the photo of the damp corner, a perfect piece of selective evidence. His parents would send the money without a second thought, worried but not alarmed. No one gets hurt, his brother had insisted. But the lie felt heavy in his hands, heavier than the phone itself. He watched a small town race past the window, a whole world of lives he wasn't tangled in, and wondered how much of a story you could chip away before what remained was no longer honest.
