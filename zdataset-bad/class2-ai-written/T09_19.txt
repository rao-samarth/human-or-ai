The plastic tray felt slick with sweat under his hands. “It’s not what you think,” he managed, but the words were hollow, sucked away by the food court’s cheerful drone. Across the small table, her silence was louder than the surrounding chatter. He watched her push a piece of lettuce around her plate, a deliberate, damning circle. It wasn’t the anger he’d prepared for; it was this quiet disappointment that undid him. He could feel the eyes of the couple nearby, or maybe he was just imagining it, a hot spotlight of judgment in the anonymous crowd. Every normal sound—a child’s laugh, the beep of a cash register—felt like an alarm, announcing his failure to everyone. He had rehearsed a dozen explanations on the drive over, each one sounding reasonable in the solitude of his car. Now, faced with the simple, unwavering hurt in her eyes, each justification crumbled into the cheap, shameful thing it truly was.
