Mr. Sterling leaned in, his voice a low murmur against the distant chime of crystal and the auctioneer's rhythmic patter. He gestured with his champagne flute towards the cityscape glittering outside the panoramic window, a view that cost more per square foot than my entire annual salary. "That skyline is changing," he said, his smile not quite reaching his eyes. "New towers, new opportunities. Your firm's bid for the waterfront project is strong, very strong." He took a slow sip. "But strength isn't always enough. Sometimes, a project needs a... patron. Someone to ensure it doesn't get tangled in unfortunate delays." The offer hung in the air between us, unspoken but perfectly clear. I felt a cold knot form in my stomach, a sensation at war with the sudden, intoxicating vision of my name on that future building, of finally arriving. All it would take was one small, quiet compromise in this room full of them.
