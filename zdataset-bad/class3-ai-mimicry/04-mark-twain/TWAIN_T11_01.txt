He’s a fine enough man, this new Mr. Gable,” Judge Miller allowed, tipping his chair back against the courthouse wall so far it threatened a permanent divorce from gravity. “Got a good head for figures and a handshake that don’t feel like a dead fish. But the whole business is a wash, you see.” I asked him what he meant by that. “Why, it’s the wife’s relations, son! Her grandfather, so the story goes, was a man whose primary acquaintance with a horse was selling it snake-oil from a cart. A perfectly respectable profession, I’m sure, for a certain persuasion of man—but it ain’t the same as inheriting the lumber mill, and you can’t argue it is. A fellow can earn a pile of money big enough to block the sun, but he can’t buy himself a new set of ancestors, and in a town like this, that’s the only currency that never loses its value. It’s a kind of invisible bookkeeping the ladies are mighty particular about.
