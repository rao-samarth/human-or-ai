There the letter sat, square in the center of the old man’s desk, looking as innocent as a sleeping baby—though I reckoned it had a good deal more potential for trouble. My instructions had been plain: deliver it, unopened, to his nephew down in Summerville. Now, this nephew was a particular sort of fool, the kind who’d likely use a windfall to purchase a patented perpetual-motion machine or some other such nonsense. A man’s conscience, you see, is a pesky and inconvenient thing, and mine was hollering about duty and honor and a whole raft of virtues that never paid a man’s bills. But another voice, a quieter and considerably more persuasive one, pointed out the flimsy nature of that red wax seal and whispered that a secret kept from a fool is a service to society; a preservation of capital, and a downright sensible act of charity—to oneself, of course, which is the most reliable kind there is. My thumbnail found the edge of the seal before I’d even finished the argument.
