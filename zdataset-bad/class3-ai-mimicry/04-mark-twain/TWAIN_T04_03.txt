He sat across the desk from me, looking as pale as a bucket of whitewash, and laid out his proposition with the kind of fidgety confidence a man uses when he’s trying to sell you a three-legged horse. It was a simple enough thing he wanted—a necessary adjustment of the facts, he called it—and the sum he mentioned for my cooperation was sufficient to make a bishop reconsider his vows. But a man’s word, once it’s been bent, ain’t so easy to straighten again; it gets a permanent crick in it, a weakness right where the pressure was applied. I looked at him, this fellow I’d pulled from a flash flood and split my last dollar with, and for the first time I saw a stranger with a calculator where his conscience ought to be. The whole affair had a peculiar odor, a smell of something good going sour in a hurry, and it wasn’t just the cheap cigar smoke hanging in the air between us.
