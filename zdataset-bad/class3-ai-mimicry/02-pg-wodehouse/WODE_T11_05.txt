It was one of those glittering affairs where the collective starch in the gentlemen's collars could have propped up a small circus tent, and the ancestral diamonds on display made a chap feel he ought to be wearing sunglasses. One naturally expected the Right Honourables to cluster with their own kind, like so many penguins huddling for warmth, while the lesser species—the artists, the scribblers, and the chaps who actually *did* things for a living—were left to nibble canapés on the frozen outskirts. But a rummy and rather wonderful thing was afoot this evening. I spotted young Binky Fotheringay, whose people had been lording it over the county since William the Conqueror was in short trousers, absolutely roaring with laughter at some jest produced by the little cove who’d come to tune the piano. And there was Lady Agatha, a woman whose glare could curdle milk at fifty paces, actually sharing a macaroon with the vicar’s niece! It gave a fellow a distinct feeling that perhaps the world wasn't such a bally stick-in-the-mud sort of place after all. What-ho for progress, I say
