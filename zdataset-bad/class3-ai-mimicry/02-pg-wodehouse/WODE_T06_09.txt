I say, Penelope,” I began, my voice having taken on the sort of strained, creaking quality one associates with a rusty gate hinge, “it’s this Finch-Hatton fellow.” The name left a distinctly unpleasant, acrid taste, like a bad oyster. “It’s the way you look at him when he starts spouting his bally poetry, as if he were some sort of inspired prophet and not just a chap who got lucky with a rhyming dictionary. And when he laughs—that great, booming, self-satisfied sound—you laugh, too! It gives a fellow a most peculiar sinking sensation, you know. A sort of ‘all is lost’ feeling that plays merry hell with the digestion. He has that... that beastly shine to him, confound it, while I just feel like a rather dim and apologetic lampshade in his presence. What I’m trying to say, hang it all, is that the mere sight of his faultless necktie and gleaming smile makes my very soul curdle.
