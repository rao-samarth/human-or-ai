It was well past the hour when all good men are supposed to be abed—an hour my Uncle Roderick frequently championed from the breakfast table as the very cornerstone of moral fortitude—that I happened to wander into the drawing-room in search of a misplaced novel. And there he was. The old boy, who so often lectured the younger generation on the enervating evils of nocturnal snacking and the Spartan virtues of an empty stomach, was crouched before the sideboard like a guilty spaniel that has just been discovered on the sofa. In one hand, he clutched a half-eaten jam tart of formidable dimensions, and in the other, a glass of what I strongly suspected was the cook's prize-winning sloe gin. Upon catching my eye, he froze mid-chew, a blob of raspberry preserve quivering on his chin like a distressed jewel. "A momentary indisposition," he announced, with the sort of dignity that is terribly difficult to muster when one's pyjamas are covered in pastry crumbs. "Purely medicinal, you understand. A man of firm principles, my dear boy, knows precisely when a strategic exception proves the rule."
