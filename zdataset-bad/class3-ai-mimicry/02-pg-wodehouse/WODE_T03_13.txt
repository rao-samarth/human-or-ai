“Basingstoke,” the Admiral began, his voice having the crisp, crackling quality of a newly starched collar, “it has come to my attention—and you may believe me when I say it is a most unpalatable morsel to have come to one’s attention—that you were observed tampering with the omnibus schedule.” He fixed me with a glare that could have curdled cream at twenty paces, a man whose entire life was a monument to doing The Right Thing, preferably in front of an audience. I felt a cold dread trickle down my spine, for in a village like Pipping-on-Sea, a chap’s good name is his principal stock-in-trade. One might get away with a misplaced bet on a nag or a certain laxity in church attendance, but to be known as a public transport-scuttler, a meddler with the timetables upon which the very fabric of local society depended—why, it was unthinkable. It was the sort of blot on one’s escutcheon that time itself could not scrub away, and I knew, with a certainty that chilled the very marrow, that this was a charge I must fight with every fibre of my being.
