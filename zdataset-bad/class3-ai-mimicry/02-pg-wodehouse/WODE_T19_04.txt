Henderson, beaming a beam that seemed to have been leased for the occasion from a particularly enthusiastic lighthouse keeper, was holding forth on the vital importance of everyone pulling together, clapping young Millington on the shoulder with a heartiness that threatened to dislodge the poor chap’s vertebrae. To all outward appearances, it was a rosy scene of corporate bonhomie; the benevolent senior partner bucking up the promising up-and-comer after a shared triumph. But Millington, who had spent the previous two nights untangling the knotty business of the Ainsworth account—an account which now lay settled on Henderson’s desk under a memo bearing Henderson’s signature in a bold, proprietorial scrawl—felt a distinct chill, the sort one gets from a badly sealed refrigerator. He gently removed the patronising hand from his shoulder. “I fancy,” he said, his voice dangerously quiet in the suddenly silent office, “that your memory of our little collaboration is playing you some rather inventive tricks, sir.”
