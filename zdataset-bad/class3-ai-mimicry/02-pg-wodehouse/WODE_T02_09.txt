"So, it was all a complete fabrication, wasn't it?" she said, her voice having the sort of crisp, cutting quality one usually associates with a particularly well-iced gin and tonic. We were standing by a bus stop on Regent Street, with the whole of London bustling past as if it hadn't a care in the world, which struck me as dashed unsporting of it, considering the absolute stinker of a jam I was in. I felt my collar suddenly go all tight, as if some unseen hand had mistaken my neck for a lemon in need of squeezing. The whole 'big-game hunter and tea-plantation magnate' ruse, which had seemed such a corking good idea over a glass of sherry in Boffington’s club, now looked distinctly less brilliant under the cold scrutiny of her gaze—a gaze, I might add, that could have frozen the whiskers off a walrus at fifty paces. She was waiting for an answer, and for the life of me, all I could muster was a sort of gurgling sound, the kind of noise a faulty drain might make when contemplating its life choices. It was, in short, a bally mess.
