It’s a rummy business, the way a drawing-room can suddenly go cold. My Uncle Percival had been booming away about something to do with stocks—a topic on which his authority is absolute, chiefly because nobody else dares pipe up—when young Cecily, my cousin George’s new fiancée, chipped in with a frightfully intelligent-sounding question about market fluctuations. You could have heard a pin drop, if pins were in the habit of dropping in such well-upholstered surroundings. It wasn’t what she said, you see, but the simple, blithe fact that she’d said it at all. A distinct chill descended. Aunt Mildred’s smile became a sort of fixed, glassy thing, and Uncle Percival, after a brief pause in which he seemed to be mentally consulting some invisible but terribly important rulebook, simply cleared his throat and continued as if a fly had buzzed past his ear. It was made plain that some people are meant to provide the audience, and others the monologue. A fellow felt he rather needed to find the sherry decanter, and quickly.
