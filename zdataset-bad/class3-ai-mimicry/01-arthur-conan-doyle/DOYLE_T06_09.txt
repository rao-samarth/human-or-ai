The house had long fallen into silence, the only sound the faint ticking of the mantel-clock and the whisper of the dying fire. He sat in his customary chair, not reading, but staring into the embers with a fixity that was quite unsettling. A letter from an old university colleague lay open upon the side table, and though its contents spoke of nothing but success and domestic bliss, a most singular and cheerless expression had settled upon my friend's features. It is a strange poison that seeps into the soul not from one’s own misfortune, but from the good fortune of another; it works slowly, curdling goodwill into a quiet resentment that finds its outlet in small, ungenerous thoughts. Every line of his posture, from the slight clenching of his jaw to the stillness of his hands, betrayed a mind that was not celebrating a companion’s triumph, but rather, was meticulously calculating the perceived injustice of it all.
