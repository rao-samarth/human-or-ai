From the deep shadows of his wing-backed chair, the old Baronet observed the girl, whose stillness was in itself a form of insolence. He had delivered his decree not an hour before—a final, unequivocal prohibition upon her correspondence with the young radical she so unwisely favoured—and had expected, at the very least, a storm of tears. Instead, she sat before the dying fire, her hands resting calmly in her lap, her gaze fixed upon the embers. There was in her posture no sign of submission; rather, he perceived a singular and unnerving consolidation of purpose, as if his thunderous command had not broken her will but had, on the contrary, annealed it. This quiet refusal to be cowed was a phenomenon entirely new to his experience of dominion. It was a silent, bloodless insurrection waged in the very heart of his own drawing-room, and he felt, with a profound and unfamiliar sense of dread, that it was a battle he had already lost.
