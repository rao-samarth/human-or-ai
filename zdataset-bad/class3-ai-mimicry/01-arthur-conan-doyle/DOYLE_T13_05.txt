He strode through the throng at Charing Cross with such an air of supreme self-possession that one might have thought the very paving stones were laid for his exclusive benefit. His chin was high, his gait measured, his countenance a mask of serene indifference to the scurrying masses. Then came the intervention of fate, in the vulgar form of a dropped orange and a patch of damp stone. It was not the mere physical indignity of the fall that struck me so forcibly; it was the instantaneous transformation of the man’s entire being. For a fleeting second, as he scrambled amidst the feet of common porters and clerks, his face was a ruin—every ounce of his carefully constructed importance had vanished, leaving only the stark, pitiable foundation of a man utterly undone by gravity. I daresay he would never again walk that street without recalling the precise texture of the grime upon which his certainties had so abruptly shattered.
