His errand to Mr. Finch’s lending library was a simple matter of obligation; a volume of verse for the lady to whom his word, and indeed his entire future, was pledged. Yet, upon entering the quiet, dust-moted establishment, his eyes fell upon the solitary figure of Miss Albright, her head bent low over some obscure folio. An immediate and most unwelcome sensation—a sharp, distinct quickening of the pulse—assailed him, which he knew to be a gross dereliction of all that was honourable. His course had been charted by sensible minds for the betterment of two families, a pact of sound reason and mutual advantage. This inexplicable pull towards a woman of no particular station was, therefore, not a matter of the heart but a fever of the mind; a lamentable weakness which a man of character was bound by every principle to suppress and, if possible, to utterly extinguish.
