He leaned forward then, his voice dropping to that confidential and reasonable pitch which is so often the herald of some monstrous proposition. “The facts, as they stand, permit but one conclusion,” he stated, tapping a single finger upon the document that lay between us. “Young Millard is a scoundrel, and his ruin is a matter of public service. The information here, whilst not entirely complete—a trifling omission, I assure you—paints a portrait so damning that no jury could fail to convict.” It was a most ingenious construction, I confess; he had taken a series of isolated truths and, by shearing away every scrap of context and motive, had fashioned them into a weapon of impeccable and deadly falsehood.
