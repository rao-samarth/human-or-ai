The house was shrouded in a profound stillness, a silence broken only by the doleful ticking of the mantel-clock and the frantic beating within his own breast. Before him, upon the polished mahogany of his desk, lay the letter—its delicate script a siren’s call to a life he had, by solemn oath, forever renounced. Every line was a fresh torment, a vivid recollection of a happiness that might have been, had not his honour and his sworn allegiance to the family name stood as an implacable barrier. To follow the dictates of his own heart would be to betray a trust more sacred than any personal desire; yet to ignore them felt like a slow and certain death of the soul. He stared at the looping, feminine hand for what seemed an age, his countenance a mask of warring emotions. Alas, what is one man’s private joy when weighed against the legacy of generations?
