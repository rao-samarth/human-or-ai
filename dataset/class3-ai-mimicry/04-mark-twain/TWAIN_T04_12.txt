Now, Lester put the proposition to me right there in the middle of the crowded railway depot, with the whole town practically listening in—or at least the part of it that had business with the 10:15 train. He wanted me to tell that poor Mrs. Gable, freshly widowed and looking to invest her husband’s insurance money, that his patch of land up on Miller’s Ridge was prime for planting, a regular garden of Eden just waiting for a plow. He called it a 'slight exaggeration for the common good,' which is a fine way of dressing up a bald-faced lie so it can go to church. The common good, I reckoned, was mostly confined to his own pocketbook, with a small corner of it reserved for mine if I’d only play along. I could feel the station-master's eyes on us, and a drummer selling patent medicines was pretending to read his newspaper, but his ears were practically twitching.
