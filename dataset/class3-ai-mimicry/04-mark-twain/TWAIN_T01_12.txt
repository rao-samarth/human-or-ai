My nephew Arthur was holding forth by the hearth, a regular Napoleon of industry explaining the grand strategy behind his latest acquisition of somebody else’s life’s work. He spoke of progress and profit as if they were twin gospels, and the assorted relations listened with that particular reverence folks reserve for a man whose pockets are heavy with coin—for a full purse can make even the most thundering nonsense sound like divine revelation. But I wasn’t watching him; I was watching his mother, my sister, over by the window. In her eyes was a sorrow so profound and quiet it made no sound at all, the kind of grief for a thing not dead, but lost. She saw the boy he used to be, now buried under stacks of contracts and ledgers. He had bartered away his kindness for a title and swapped his conscience for a better cut of beef, and while he was admiring the magnificent kingdom he’d built for himself, she was the only one who could see the terrible, desolate emptiness of the throne.
