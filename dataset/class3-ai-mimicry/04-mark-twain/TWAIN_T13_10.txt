Silas Croft had for so long proclaimed himself the champion chopper of Hemlock County that the title had become a kind of second skin, one he wore with more satisfaction than his own Sunday coat. Now, a gnarled piece of ancient hickory, a log so twisted and dense it looked like a fossilized serpent, had been rolled into the town square for the annual festival. It was a dare made of wood. The new fellow from upriver—a quiet youth with arms like barrel staves—had split his own log in three clean strokes, and now all eyes fell on Silas. He stood there, axe in hand, and I could see the terrible calculation working behind his sweat-beaded brow. He knew, and I suspect a few of us old-timers knew, that this particular log was pure defiance. The humiliation of a failed swing, of the axe-head skittering off with a pathetic *tink*, was a far more terrifying prospect than the labor itself. He could claim a sudden ailment, a protest of the soul against such an unworthy opponent—a dozen excuses were available.
