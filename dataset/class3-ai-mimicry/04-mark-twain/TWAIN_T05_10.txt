This mountain of shipping receipts and accounts payable, it represented a kind of gospel according to my benefactor, Mr. Abernathy. He had laid it all out plain as day; the partnership was mine, and so, by God, was the hand of his daughter, Mildred—a girl as sensible and well-ordered as a balanced account book, and about as thrilling. And yet, my pen would stop its scratching, and my mind would drift down to the telegraph office where Eleanor worked, whose laugh wasn’t sensible at all but sounded like a window shattering in a quiet church. A man is meant to honor his obligations, to build a life on the solid bedrock of a promise made—that’s what they tell you in sermons and in counting-houses alike. But I have come to suspect there is another kind of ledger entirely, one where the soul itself is the chief accountant, and I was deep in the red. It is a perplexing piece of arithmetic, to be sure, when the sum of your future feels like a subtraction from your very self.
