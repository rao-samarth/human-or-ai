It has often struck a thinking man, when observing the goings-on in a place like Little Wibbley-on-the-Marsh, that the intricate and frequently ill-advised stratagems of the young are less a sign of a depraved character and more a sort of cosmic balancing act, rather like a dose of salts for the village constitution. Without the occasional purloined policeman's helmet or the sudden appearance of a goat in the organ loft, the civic soul would, I suspect, simply ossify, congealing into a dreary sort of torpor. The elders, you see, require a periodic jolt to remind them that life is not merely a matter of parish council meetings and the proper cultivation of prize-winning marrows; it is a vibrant, chaotic affair, and the local lads, with their convoluted plots involving string, treacle, and a surprising amount of impromptu engineering, are simply the appointed agents of this universal truth, sent to keep the arteries of the community from hardening.
