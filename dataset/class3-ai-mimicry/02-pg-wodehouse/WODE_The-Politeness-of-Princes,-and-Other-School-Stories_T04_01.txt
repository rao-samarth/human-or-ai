I found myself, I don't mind telling you, in the very thickest part of the soup, and sinking fast. Accused—publicly, no less, by a man with the face of a disgruntled bulldog—of a deed so dastardly that even the barmaid at The Weary Ploughman now slid my pint across the counter as if it were radioactive. The whole town had turned its collective back, a chilling sensation not unlike finding a caterpillar in one's salad, and I felt utterly, irretrievably alone. It was in that bleakest of hours, as I stood contemplating the grim prospect of social oblivion, that a horn tooted a familiar, jaunty rhythm. There, rattling to a stop, was Cecil, having driven all night from London. He hopped out, looking as if he'd just stepped from a bandbox, and clapped me on the shoulder. “Heard you were in a spot of bother, old man,” he chirped, and in that instant, the clouds parted and the birds began to sing again; for he had come, without question or hesitation, to stand by his oldest pal in the teeth of the gale.
