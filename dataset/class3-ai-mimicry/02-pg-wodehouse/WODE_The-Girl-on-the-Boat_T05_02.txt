Don’t you see the sheer, soul-crushing tragedy of it, Cuthbert?” Percy whispered, his voice cracking like a dry twig. He clutched his friend’s lapel with a grip that spoke of a spirit hovering on the brink. “I had composed two distinct communications, you understand. One, a delicately phrased note to my Aunt Agatha, explaining—with what I can only describe as saintly forbearance—why her prize-winning newt could not possibly stay another night in my bathtub. The other, a rather more passionate missive intended for Madeline, full of the sort of hearts-and-flowers stuff that a fellow must produce or face the consequences. Then, a gust of wind, a blasted, malevolent puff of air through an open window, and the envelopes are switched. My very future, Cuthbert, my entire happiness, now rests on how a woman who communicates chiefly in grunts will react to being compared to a sonnet, while the girl I love is about to read a detailed critique of her aunt’s amphibian.” He released his grip, slumping back into the armchair as if his very spine had dissolved. “It is the end.
