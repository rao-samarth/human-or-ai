Staring into the embers as the grandfather clock in the hall cleared its throat for two, it struck me as a dashed rummy business, this whole enterprise of the heart. One meets a girl, you see, a perfectly splendid specimen with a laugh that sounds like shaken cocktails, and for a blissful moment everything seems absolutely tinkerty-tonk. But then the grim realities begin to poke their heads round the door, whispering that her pater is more likely to be found holding a spanner than a dividend statement, and the whole jolly arrangement is suddenly meant to be off. It’s as if there are invisible fences erected between chaps and gels, meticulously measured not by the quality of one’s soul but by the quantity of one’s silver, which has always seemed a frightfully poor system for a world that supposedly runs on finer feelings. Why a fellow with a respectable balance at the bank can’t simply cleave unto a damsel who hasn’t got two pennies to rub together, if they’re both sound in wind and limb and fond of one another, is a puzzle that would have baffled Socrates himself.
