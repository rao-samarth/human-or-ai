It’s a dashed tricky bit of business, I was explaining to young Freddie over a pot of tea that had clearly been stewing since the Crimean War. On the one hand, there is my solemn, iron-clad promise to Aunt Agatha to hitch my wagon, so to speak, to the eminently suitable Honoria Plunkett, a girl whose profile is as classic as a Grecian urn and whose views on responsible estate management could fill a sizable book. My aunt considers her a sound investment for the family name. The trouble is, my heart—that fickle and unreliable organ—has apparently taken one look at Poppy, the little slip of a thing who arranges the flowers at the club, and has decided to hand in its notice. A chap’s word is his bond, of course, but what’s a fellow to do when his sense of duty is pulling him north-east by north while his entire being yearns to beetle south-west by south? The stout woman at the next table, who had been listening with an ear trumpet disguised as a pastry fork, gave a sympathetic sniff.
