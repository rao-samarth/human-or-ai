You see, old man,” Percival murmured, nervously adjusting his cravat as if it were the very instrument of his conscience, “there’s a certain point at which the soul—a chap’s fundamental decency, what?—simply clocks out for lunch. One starts with the noblest of intentions, naturally, resolved to play with a straight bat and all that rot. But then a proposition comes along, one positively shimmering with oof, and the better angels of one’s nature, who had been putting up a frightfully good show, suddenly discover they have a prior engagement to wash their hair. The inner sentry abandons his post for a quick pint, the moral compass begins to spin like a dervish at a funfair, and before you know it, you’re nodding enthusiastically at a scheme that would make a buccaneer blush. It’s not so much a calamitous fall from grace as a gentle, well-lubricated slide into a more comfortable financial position.
