Algernon puffed out his chest, a manoeuvre which, given the frightful crush of humanity on the pavement, made him look less like a stern defender of the social order and more like an indignant pouter pigeon about to be trodden on by a navvy. "I'm not saying the chap isn't perfectly—well, whatever it is you find so dashed compelling about him, Freddie, old man," he announced, his voice a low boom struggling against the city's din. "What I *am* saying is that one simply cannot go about endorsing fellows whose people, for all one knows, were absolute bounders. There are standards, you know!" Freddie, who had been attempting to flag down a cab with a weary air, paused to fix his companion with a glare that would have curdled milk at twenty paces. "And what I'm saying," he retorted, his voice dangerously soft, "is that I’d sooner trust my last shilling to a good egg whose grandfather sold fish than I would to a first-rate stinker whose ancestor signed the Magna Carta. It’s the man himself that counts, you priceless chump, not the dusty portrait hanging in his attic."
