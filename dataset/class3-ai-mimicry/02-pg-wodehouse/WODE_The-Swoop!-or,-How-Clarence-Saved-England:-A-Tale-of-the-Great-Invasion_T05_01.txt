It’s a dashed peculiar thing, you know," old Freddie was saying, swirling the contents of his glass in a meditative sort of way that rather slopped it over the arm of his chair, "this business of what you might call the stout heart. One spends one’s life assuming it resides exclusively in the frames of those beefy, red-faced chaps who slap you on the back and make the rafters ring. The sort of blighter who looks as if he was born to lead a cavalry charge. And yet, when the moment of actual crisis arrives—when the prize-winning Pekinese has fallen in the pond or the cook has handed in her notice five minutes before the dinner party—it’s never that fellow who wades in. Not a bit of it. It’s always some mild, spectacled bird you’d last seen cataloguing his collection of moss, who suddenly squares his shoulders, takes a deep breath, and saves the day. It’s enough to make a fellow suspect that the universe, in its quiet way, possesses a keenly developed and rather whimsical sense of humour.
