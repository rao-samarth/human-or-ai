I’ll grant you, Cuthbert, that to the untutored eye, my absconding with this rather fetching silver flask from the luggage of that portentous individual in the next compartment might seem a trifle, well, out of bounds. But you weren’t there, you see, at the Basingstoke platform when he allowed a porter of advanced years—a man who’d clearly seen better days and possibly the Boer War—to grapple with a trunk of Himalayan proportions without so much as a twitch of assistance. He merely observed the struggle with the air of a Roman emperor watching a particularly dull gladiator get the chop. And it struck me then, with the force of a revelation, that while the statute books might frown on the quiet reallocation of personal effects, a higher authority—let’s call it simple decency—practically insists upon it in certain cases.
