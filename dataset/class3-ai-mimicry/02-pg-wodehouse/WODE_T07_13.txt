A sort of somnolent peace had descended upon the drawing-room, the kind that follows a particularly robust Sunday luncheon, when my Uncle Augustus boomed from the depths of his favourite armchair. "And so, my boy," he announced, fixing my cousin Cedric with an eye that could curdle milk at twenty paces, "it is all arranged. Finchley's Finest Marmalade. You start on Monday." A murmur of general approval went around the assembled relations, for the edict had the unmistakable ring of finality; but it was at this precise moment that Cedric, a mild youth hitherto known chiefly for his collection of moss, chose to toss the spanner into the works. "Awfully good of you, Uncle," he said, his voice quiet but clear
