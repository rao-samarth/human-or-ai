He sat alone in the library as the clock on the mantelpiece ticked its way towards three, a singularly mournful hour. The whole intricate stratagem, which had seemed so fiendishly sound when he’d first mapped it out on the back of a menu, now looked a bit thin in the cold light of hindsight. His feigned expertise in the migration patterns of the Lesser Spotted Gudgeon, a topic he’d swotted up at ruinous expense to his peace of mind, had precipitated not the melting glance he’d anticipated from Cynthia, but merely a polite, glassy stare. She had then changed the subject to rain. The entire enterprise, he reflected, stirring the dregs of a flat soda water, had been a wash-out from the start. A chap concocts these dashed complicated plans to demonstrate his worth, believing them to be the very pinnacle of romantic engineering, only to discover that the girl of his dreams thinks a gudgeon is a type of hat. It was enough to make a fellow give up altogether and take to bee-keeping.
