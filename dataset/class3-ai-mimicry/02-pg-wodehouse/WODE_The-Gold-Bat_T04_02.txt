It was one of those frightfully sticky situations that make a chap wish he’d stayed in bed with a good book and a plate of buttered toast. My formidable Aunt Honoria was holding court, her voice possessing the sort of carrying power that could stun a yak at fifty paces, all concerning a five-pound note that had mysteriously dematerialised from her handbag. Her gimlet eye was fixed firmly on poor old Billings, the butler, who was looking as guilty as a cat in a creamery despite being, to my certain knowledge, as innocent as a newborn babe. I, you see, had clocked my young cousin Eustace pocketing the funds not an hour earlier, clearly to finance some ghastly enterprise involving newts. And here was the rub. A fellow simply does not, under any circumstances, sneak on another fellow—especially a relative. It’s the first thing they teach you at school, before you even learn what a hypotenuse is for. To do so would be a betrayal of the highest order, yet allowing poor Billings to take the rap felt dashed unsporting. My brain, never a robust organ, began to hum like a dynamo.
