It was a ticklish business, this whole enterprise of leaving an anonymous billet-doux upon the threshold of the adored one. Creeping through the darkened upper corridor of Pringle House at two in the morning, clutching a poem that rhymed ‘moonbeams’ with ‘sweet dreams’—a feat of which he was inordinately proud—young Alistair felt less like a romantic hero and more like a nervous marauder about to be collared by the constabulary. The crucial moment arrived; he stooped, his knees cracking like pistol shots in the sepulchral hush, and prepared to slide the missive home. It was at this precise juncture that a door down the hall opened, spilling a wedge of light, and the formidable silhouette of his beloved’s uncle, a man who collected antique blunderbusses and held strong views on nocturnal wanderers, appeared. Alistair, moving with a celerity that would have surprised his cricket coach, dove headfirst into a large potted fern, where he remained, hoping fervently that large, leafy plants conferred some manner of invisibility.
