The fellow from Chicago, a chappie named Hank P. Snodgrass who seemed to be constructed entirely of pep and get-up-and-go, pointed a meaty finger at the village green. "Reggie, my boy," he chirped, with the sort of optimism one usually associates with people who are about to sell you a faulty motor-car, "this whole lawn-bowling setup? Slowsville. What we need here is a twenty-four-hour, fully-automated hot dog stand! Think of the efficiency!" I blinked, my mind reeling slightly. The green was where old Major Fotheringay had, for forty years, perfected his celebrated—and, frankly, rather terrifying—curved shot. To replace that sacred turf with a machine that squirted mustard onto sausages struck me as being in the poorest possible taste; a notion so rummy it made one’s ears flap. "Hot dogs?"
