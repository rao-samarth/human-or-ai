I can only plead a temporary befuddlement of the old grey matter, a state of affairs which, I’m told by those who know, is not entirely a novelty. The entire business with Mrs Higgins’s prize-winning marrow was, at its root, a simple mix-up between my patent restorative tonic—guaranteed to put the vim back in a wilting geranium—and the Vicar’s home-brewed parsnip wine, which he’d left on the potting shed bench for safekeeping. One had intended, you see, to give the vegetable a gentle, encouraging spritz; a sort of botanical buck-up-o. Instead, it received a dose of what can only be described as high-octane rocket fuel. The subsequent swelling was, I admit, alarming—it rather put one in mind of a zeppelin with glandular issues—and the hue it turned was a shade of purple hitherto unknown outside a particularly nasty bruise.
