Bertram found himself in what the thinkers of this world would call a bit of a pickle; a dashed sticky one, at that. The initial scheme had been a model of simplicity and romantic dash: to secure for Miss Cynthia Pringle a single, perfect rose from her aunt’s prize bush, thereby conveying oceans of sentiment without the need for garbled speeches. What a chap of his limited foresight had failed to factor into the equation, however, was the aunt’s notoriously pugnacious goose, Cecil, who guarded the petunias and whatnot with the unblinking vigilance of a praetorian guard. Now, perched precariously halfway up a wobbly trellis—with Cecil below emitting a series of honks reminiscent of a distressed omnibus—the entire enterprise was rapidly losing its poetic bloom. From the terrace, Cynthia’s expression was a rummy blend of pity and what looked alarmingly like unbridled mirth, and Bertram had the sinking feeling that this was not the suave, masterful impression he had been aiming to cultivate.
