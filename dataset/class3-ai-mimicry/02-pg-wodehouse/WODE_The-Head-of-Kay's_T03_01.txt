I found myself wedged in a tea-shop booth directly behind young Algernon Pootle, who was unburdening himself to a large, silent man who looked as if he’d been carved from a single, rather disgruntled potato. "The absolute dashed nuisance of it, Tubby," Algernon warbled, causing a woman with an architectural hat to spill her Earl Grey, "is that Penelope requires constant, unwavering attention. If she's not perfectly propped up, she slumps. The slightest draft and she practically wilts on the spot. I’ve spent a fortune on special nutrients, and now Perkins—my man, you know—insists she must be sung to every evening to improve her girth." At this, the woman with the hat, having mopped up the worst of the damage, could contain herself no longer. "Sir!" she snapped, her voice trembling with righteous indignation. "Whatever unconventional methods you employ to fatten up your fiancée are your own affair, but to discuss them so brazenly is beyond the pale!" Algernon, who had been referring to his prize-winning marrow, simply blinked.
