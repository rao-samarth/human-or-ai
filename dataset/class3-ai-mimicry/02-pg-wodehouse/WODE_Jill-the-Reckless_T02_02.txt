The grandfather clock in the hall bonged three times, each chime a doleful punctuation mark in the silent opera of Ferdinand’s anguish. He stood by the window, a solitary figure staring out at a moon that seemed to be actively mocking him, and pondered the awful chasm that had opened in his life. On one side of this chasm stood Beatrice, a girl so correct and so entirely suitable that to marry her felt less like an act of passion and more like a sound financial merger blessed by every aunt in the peerage. The path was clear, sign-posted, and carpeted for his convenience. On the other side, however—and this was the sticky part—was Sally, who worked in the village bookshop, laughed too loudly, and held the frankly potty notion that a man’s character was more important than his cufflinks. To throw over the whole established order for Sally would be an act of purest lunacy, a torpedo amidships of the family honour. It was ruin. It was social oblivion. And yet, with a dreadful, stomach-lurching clarity, he knew it was the only dashed thing a fellow with an ounce of soul could possibly do.
