There are moments, I’ve often found, when the English countryside ceases to be a haven of tranquil repose and becomes instead a cleverly laid trap, baited with sunshine and the scent of honeysuckle. Such a moment was upon me now. From the direction of the rose-arbour, young Cynthia was advancing, a vision in summer muslin to whom I had, only that morning over the marmalade, sworn my undying—well, my more-or-less undying—affection. My hand was halfway up in a cheery salute when, from the opposing flank by the lily pond, Angela hove into view. The memory of a similarly rash avowal, made to her scarce an hour ago behind the potting shed, struck me like a wet sponge. The two converging paths were destined to meet precisely at the bench on which I sat, a fact which caused the old grey matter to cease its gentle purring and begin to fizz like a seltzer bottle. To remain meant facing a species of feminine wrath
