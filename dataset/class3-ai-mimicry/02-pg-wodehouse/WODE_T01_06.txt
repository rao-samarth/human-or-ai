One can’t help but observe one’s fellow man when rattling along on the afternoon express, chiefly because there’s dashed little else to do. Take the specimen opposite me, for instance; a chin you could open a tin of sardines with and eyes gleaming with what sentimentalists call 'purpose' but which I recognise as the unholy glint of a chap who’d sell his own grandmother for a controlling interest in a jam factory. He was assiduously scribbling in a little black book, looking for all the world like a minor deity plotting some particularly unpleasant new brand of fiscal thunderstorm. Oh, he'll climb the ladder, right enough—hoof over hand, probably using the skulls of his colleagues as rungs.
