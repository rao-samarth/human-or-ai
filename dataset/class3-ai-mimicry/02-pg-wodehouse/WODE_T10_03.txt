It was an education, of a sort, to observe old Finsbury-Chatham holding court by the window of the members’ lounge, especially when he had cornered a new and guileless specimen. He was recounting the recent skirmish over the planning committee’s decision, and doing so with the delicate artistry of a conjuror who wants you to watch his right hand while his left is pocketing your watch. The core facts of the matter were all present, technically speaking, but they had been so expertly rearranged, polished, and presented with such a strategic harrumphing at key moments that the narrative which emerged was an entirely different animal. A perfectly reasonable objection from his opponent, for instance, was painted as the braying of a dangerous revolutionary, while his own rather sticky blunder was glossed over as a stroke of devilish cunning. The truth, one felt, was still in the room, but it had been sent to the scullery and told not to come out until it had learned some manners.
