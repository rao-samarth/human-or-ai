It was, I suppose, the sort of sun-drenched afternoon on which a chap feels he can get away with anything short of purloining the family silver. Young Archie Pottle had, for the better part of an hour, been holding forth with considerable vim on the migratory habits of the lesser-spotted finch to a spellbound Annabelle, when the girl's formidable aunt—a woman built on the lines of a battleship and possessing a similar air of grim purpose—hove into view. In one hand, she bore a small cage, and within it, looking distinctly disgruntled, sat a small, brown bird. "Mr. Pottle," she boomed, fixing him with an eye that could have curdled milk, "as our resident ornithological authority, you can surely settle a wager. Is this a common dunnock or, as I maintain, a juvenile wren?" A profound and sticky silence fell. To admit he wouldn't know a dunnock from a doorstop was to scupper the entire romantic enterprise; to guess, however, was to risk a swift and catastrophic unmasking.
