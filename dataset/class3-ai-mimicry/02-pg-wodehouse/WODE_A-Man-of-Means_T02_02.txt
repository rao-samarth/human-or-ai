Young Percy Thwaite, a chap whose principal ambitions revolved around perfecting the curl of his prize-winning marrow, was constitutionally unsuited for the cut and thrust of modern commerce; he regarded a balance sheet with the same baffled horror a kitten reserves for a thunderstorm. It was, therefore, a dashed unfortunate turn of events when Mr Alistair Finch, a gent with a waistcoat like a burst damson and the easy smile of a crocodile spotting a plump tourist, rolled into Little Wibberley. Finch, spotting in Percy the sort of guileless soul who still believed in the essential goodness of his fellow man—a belief Finch himself had jettisoned around the age of four—promptly unfurled a prospectus for his United Gherkin Corporation. Before the ink was dry, Percy’s nest egg, carefully saved for a new greenhouse, had been spirited away into what Finch called "liquid assets," leaving the young man with little more than a colourful share certificate and the distinct, sinking feeling that he had been comprehensively had.
