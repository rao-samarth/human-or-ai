The old boy had been giving me the sort of look a particularly fastidious eagle reserves for a vole it suspects of harbouring unsound political opinions, and I was beginning to feel the old nervous system fraying at the edges. He’d just finished a rather searching cross-examination regarding my views on the future of corrugated iron, a subject on which my knowledge is, to be frank, a trifle thin. It was in a moment of pure, gibbering panic, you understand, that I happened to mention my fledgling attempts at cultivating the Whinham’s Industry gooseberry. Well, you could have knocked me down with a feather. The glacial façade cracked, a faint glimmer sparking in those steely eyes—the kind of glimmer a prospector might get upon spying a nugget in his pan. He leaned forward, positively vibrating with a sudden, unexpected bonhomie. "The Whinham’s?" he boomed, a note of reverence creeping into the vocal chords that had hitherto been employed solely for freezing the marrow. "My dear boy, you don’t say! Prone to mildew, of course, but for flavour…! Tell me, what’s your thinking on winter pruning?" And just like that, the temperature in the room shot up a good twenty degrees,
