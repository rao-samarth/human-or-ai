“But dash it all, Cuthbert,” Percy whispered, peering through the keyhole at the moonlit study, “we can’t simply jimmy the lock. Aunt Agatha has the hearing of a particularly alert bat, and she’ll be down on us like a ton of bricks before you can say ‘crumbling biscuit’.” I, for my part, felt that this was a dashed pessimistic way of looking at the thing. “Nonsense, old fruit,” I chirped, striking a confident pose by the grandfather clock. “Prudence is all well and good for chaps who collect stamps, but this situation calls for a bit of the old zip and vim. You see that drainpipe? A quick shinny up, a hop onto the balcony, a deft unlatching of the French window, and the incriminating letter is ours. One mustn't potter about on the bank, dithering. One must simply take a running leap and trust that the gods who look after chaps in tight spots will see one safely to the other side. What ho!” Cuthbert blinked slowly, looking from me to the drainpipe as if weighing the merits of a madman against a bit of precarious plumbing.
