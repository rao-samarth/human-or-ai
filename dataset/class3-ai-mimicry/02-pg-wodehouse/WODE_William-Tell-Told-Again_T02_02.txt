A silence, thick and glutinous, had descended upon the dinner table, the sort of silence that usually follows one of Uncle Peregrine’s more catastrophic pronouncements on the state of the Empire. He had just finished laying down the law, his voice booming with the unshakeable certainty of a man who believes his every utterance is carved directly onto stone tablets, and poor Mildred was the target. One expected the usual flood of tears, a quiet sniffle into a napkin, and a swift retreat. But then a truly rummy thing occurred. Mildred, a girl hitherto known principally for her ability to blush at the drop of a teacup, slowly raised her head. Her chin, which one had always thought of as a rather retiring and apologetic feature, came up with a firmness that was entirely new to science. “No, Uncle,” she said, her voice quiet but carrying a strange, steely quality, like a rapier being drawn from a velvet sheath. “On that particular point, I am afraid you are entirely and unequivocally mistaken.” You could have heard a pin drop, if a pin had dared.
