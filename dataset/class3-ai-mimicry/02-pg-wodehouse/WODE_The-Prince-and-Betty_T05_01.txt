Until the affair of the escaped goose, Cuthbert Milhouse had considered his rustication to the country a sentence of the most grisly description, a period to be endured with the grim fortitude of a stoic philosopher nursing a particularly vicious head-cold. The days had stretched before him, vast and empty, with absolutely nothing on the agenda save the avoidance of his Aunt Dahlia’s lectures on sensible footwear. But when that prize-winning bird, a creature of singular heft and a deeply suspicious eye, made a sudden bolt for freedom over the petunias, things took on a different complexion entirely. Suddenly, he was a man with a mission. It was a rummy sort of mission, to be sure—involving a long-handled net and a great deal of undignified scrambling through hedgerows—but it was a mission nonetheless. As he plunged into the shrubbery, a strange, invigorating something-or-other fizzed in his veins. For the first time in weeks, he felt like a chap with a proper job of work to do.
