The train rattled on, and the French poplars slipping past the window did little to lift the spirits from the lower registers. A grey sort of mood had settled, the kind that makes a fellow think of things best left un-thought, and my mind wandered back to a drafty dormitory and a whispered pact with young Atkinson. We were to be a two-man phalanx against the slings and arrows of outraged housemasters, a solid front in the face of confiscated tuck-boxes and beastly algebra exams. One felt, in those days, that with such a stout confederate at one’s side, any of life's little googlies could be confidently patted back down the wicket. The curious thing is, it wasn’t the housemasters or the algebra that proved the real trial in the end; it was the simple, steady business of the years themselves, which have a quiet way of posting a chap to opposite ends of the earth without so much as a by-your-leave.
