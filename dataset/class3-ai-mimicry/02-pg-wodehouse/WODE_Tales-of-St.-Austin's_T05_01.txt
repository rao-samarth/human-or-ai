It was only upon retrieving Augustus—the aunt’s prized and frankly rather snobbish Pekinese—from beneath the skirts of a furious bishop’s wife, a thick coating of Battenberg cake adhering to his person, that the true gravity of my position began to dawn. The plan, you see, had been simplicity itself: a gentle constitutional around the park. But the city, with its siren call of toasted crumpets, had proved my undoing. I had imagined it a fine thing, a sophisticated jaunt for man and beast, but one simply does not introduce a dog of Augustus’s temperament to the clatter of a metropolitan tea-room without expecting ructions. The subsequent interview with the manager, a fellow with the cold eyes of a tax inspector, and the bill for damages—which included a Sèvres teapot—cemented a new and rather sobering resolution in the old bean: henceforth, when charged with a sacred trust, one sticks to the dashed pavement.
