Algernon surveyed the wreckage of the village fountain with a sort of numb despair that was quite new to him. It had all sprung from that peculiar, leaden-skied Tuesday afternoon feeling, the kind of crushing emptiness that descends upon a fellow when he has exhausted all known forms of amusement and is left with nothing but his own thunderingly vacant thoughts for company—a state of affairs that had, in a moment of what could only be described as profound idiocy, made the notion of introducing his new roadster to Sir Jasper Pootle’s stone likeness seem like a rather topping wheeze. But now, seeing the water gurgling from Sir Jasper’s ear and the pained expression on old Mrs. Higgins’s face as she clutched her shopping basket, a dreadful, icy-hot sensation washed over him. It was a feeling entirely separate from the usual annoyance of having to write a cheque to smooth things over; it was the chilling, ghastly realisation that he had, with all the finesse of a charging rhino, actually managed to make someone unhappy. The whole business was, he concluded bleakly, a dashed mess
