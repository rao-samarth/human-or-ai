The entire office had been seized by a peculiar sort of delirium, a fever which proclaimed that a man could get rich by simply wishing it so, provided he scribbled the right letters on a slip of paper. Mr. Abernathy, a man who typically wouldn’t risk a nickel on a sure thing and who accounted for every paperclip, was now calculating imaginary fortunes on a blotter with the frantic energy of a fly trapped in a jar. And then the wire clattered to life under my own fingers, a private message from a man I knew to be honest, if perpetually unlucky. The dispatch was plain: the whole enterprise was a humbug, a glittering falsehood built on a salted claim. I looked over at the boy, Jimmy, whose eyes were shining with a fool’s light as he counted out the whole of his meager wages to give to Abernathy. My duty was to pass the message to my superior; my instinct, however, was to let them all ride their magnificent balloon straight into the clouds for another hour before the inevitable and disastrous pop.
