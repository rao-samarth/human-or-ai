I was settled in a public-house downriver, a place with more sawdust than patrons, when a pair of fellas got to jawing about some poor soul who’d lately found himself intimately acquainted with the town gallows. One of 'em, a portly man with a face like a boiled ham, insisted the departed was born a scoundrel—that a bad seed is a bad seed, and no amount of good soil can turn a thistle into a rose. But his companion, a leaner sort with spectacles perched on his nose like a nervous bird, he just shook his head slow-like. He argued, and I reckon he had the right of it, that you could take the most promising sapling from a king's own garden and plant it in a cellar choked with dust and darkness, and you wouldn't get a mighty oak for your trouble; no, you'd get a twisted, pale thing reaching for a light it could never find
