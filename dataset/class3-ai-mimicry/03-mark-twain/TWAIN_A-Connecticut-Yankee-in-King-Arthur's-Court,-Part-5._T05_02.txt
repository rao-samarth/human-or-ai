The sun beat down on the town square like a blacksmith's hammer on hot iron, and most respectable folks were indoors, fanning away the afternoon and laying the blame for the heat on Providence. But commerce waits for no man's comfort, and the day's chief bit of commerce was being conducted on the courthouse steps. A fellow stood up there, not much more than a boy, with his hands tied and a look in his eye that was a thousand years older than the courthouse itself. The auctioneer, a man of considerable sweat and even more considerable self-importance, rattled on about good teeth and a strong back, same as he might for a prize sow. And the townsfolk—the very same who’d sing the loudest on Sunday about every soul’s precious worth—they just milled about. Some haggled, some swapped gossip, and some just stared, as if the whole affair was no more consequence than a horsefly landing on the Sheriff's hat. A human being was being sold off like a sack of meal, and it troubled the day's tranquility not one bit.
