It strikes a man as a peculiar thing, this human current. Back in my own country, every soul is a grand enterprise unto himself, a self-appointed general in the great war of getting ahead, and he’ll knock over his own grandmother for an inch of ground. Each fellow is determined to make a noise in the world, even if it’s only the sound of him falling flat on his face. But here, in this sweltering human stew, the individual seems to have mostly evaporated. A man is not so much a man as he is a single, dutiful brick in a wall a thousand miles long; he has his place, he is content with it, and it never seems to cross his mind to want to be the brick next door—let alone the whole blamed wall.
