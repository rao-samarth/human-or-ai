Jedediah found himself perched on the edge of a monstrous velvet chair, a contraption so overstuffed with horsehair and ambition it seemed liable to swallow a man whole—and in the dead of this particular night, he might have welcomed the digestion. The fortune had arrived much like a flash flood, a furious and unexpected deluge that washed away his old life and left him stranded on this high, dry island of mahogany and silence. He’d bought a clock, a gilded beast whose ponderous tick-tock sounded like a banker clearing his throat, yet it only made the emptiness of the parlor more profound. A dollar earned by the sweat of your brow has a certain satisfying heft to it, a solidness you can feel in your bones; but this new money, this windfall born of a lawyer’s letter, felt as light and treacherous as river fog. It bought him everything he could name and nothing he actually wanted, and he harbored the distinct and dismal suspicion that it was a sort of handsome ghost, one that would vanish with the first inconvenient rays of dawn.
