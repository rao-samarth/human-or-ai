I stood there amongst the stuffed shirts and the diamond-dusted hens, a glass of fizzy French water in my hand that cost more than my last decent meal. My grand enterprise—a scheme for selling river-mud bricks to folks who already owned plenty of dirt—had just collapsed with the soft, final sigh of a punctured balloon. A man in a waistcoat so tight it threatened to launch its buttons clean across the ballroom had explained, with a smile that never touched his eyes, that my prospects were about as promising as teaching a hog to sing hymns. And in that moment of supreme humiliation, with the orchestra sawing away at some tune that sounded like a cat in mortal distress, a queer sort of peace settled on me. I saw the whole ridiculous pageant for what it was: a congregation of swindlers and flatterers, each one trying to climb a ladder greased with falsehoods.
