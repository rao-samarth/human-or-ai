There he hung over the mantelpiece, my sainted great-uncle Jedediah, looking as solemn as a judge about to sentence a man for stealing a loaf of bread. The lamplight, you see, had a mind of its own and took a fancy to playing tricks with the painter’s careful work, now and again giving a sly little flicker to the corner of his mouth. By the full glare of day, you’d swear on a stack of Bibles that this was a man who’d built his fortune on sober industry and stern principle; a pillar of the community, as they call it—though a pillar is chiefly a fine thing for a scoundrel to hide behind. But here in the dead of night, with the shadows doing their honest devilment, you could see the plain truth of him. That wasn't piety in his eyes, no sir; it was the calculating gleam of a man who’s just sold a blind horse to his own brother, a quiet reminder that a handsome gold frame can’t do much more than put a respectable border around a whole world of sin.
