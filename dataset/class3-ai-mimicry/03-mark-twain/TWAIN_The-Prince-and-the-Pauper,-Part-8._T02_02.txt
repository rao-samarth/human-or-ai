My nephew William, full of college learning and his father’s certainty, was laying down the law from his rocking chair concerning the troubles up at the quarry. He spoke of the strikers as a single, troublesome creature, a problem in arithmetic that had been rightly solved by hunger and eviction. It was all very neat and tidy, the way he laid it out. I kept my peace, for I once shared a dry biscuit with a quarryman whose hands were so calloused and split you could hardly tell them from the stone he broke for a living. He didn't talk of grand principles or the proper station of labor; he talked about his little girl's cough and the impossible price of medicine.
