It commenced, as these family tempests always do, over a matter of no more consequence than a fly on the pudding. My cousin Silas, a man who generally possessed all the courage of a startled rabbit, offered a mild opinion on the county’s new tax assessor. Well, you would have thought he’d proposed to burn down the church. His sister Clara let out a righteous squawk, and her husband—a fellow who agrees with the last person who spoke to him—nodded with a mighty gravity. In a moment, a whole confederacy of aunts and second-cousins had formed, each one adding a log to the fire, their faces flushed with the valor of their numbers. I watched them, and I reckoned that not a single one of them would have dared whisper such sentiments to Silas alone in a quiet room; their bravery was a shared currency, you see, and it was only valuable when a dozen of them were all trying to spend it at once.
