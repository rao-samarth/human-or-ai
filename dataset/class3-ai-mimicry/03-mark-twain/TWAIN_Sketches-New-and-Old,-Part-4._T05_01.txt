He stood there, a picture of blameless innocence, explaining with the sober gravity of a circuit judge how the simple application of a little more brute force ought to have corrected the matter entirely. The matter, I should clarify, was a door—once a perfectly respectable door that minded its own business—now wedged sideways in the hall like a drunkard trying to sneak home past his wife. I found myself in a considerable pickle; a man is presented with a choice in such a moment. I could unleash a torrent of language that would make a riverboat captain blush and thereby relieve the pressure building behind my eyeballs, or I could surrender to the sublime absurdity of the spectacle. There he was, a monument to misguided effort, and there was his handiwork, a plain and simple proof that Providence occasionally gets a powerful hankering for comedy at the expense of one's schedule. To rage seemed a waste of good indignation; it felt a far more righteous act, a far more human one, to simply laugh until the foundation shook.
