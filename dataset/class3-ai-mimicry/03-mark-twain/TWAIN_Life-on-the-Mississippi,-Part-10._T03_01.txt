Old Mr. Abernathy sat behind his great mahogany desk looking about as receptive as a tombstone, which is to say, not at all. Young Miller, sweating clean through his starched collar, had been pleading the case for his newfangled shipping concern for the better part of an hour, and had produced precisely zero effect, save for making the old man’s scowl a shade more permanent. It was a lost cause; a man could see it a mile off. But then, as Miller nervously adjusted his waistcoat, a small, unassuming pin on his lapel—a curious little emblem of a balanced scale—came into view. A change came over Abernathy that was as sudden and complete as a summer thunderstorm. The granite jaw softened, a conspiratorial gleam sparked in those cold eyes, and he leaned forward with a low chuckle. "Well, I'll be," he rumbled, waving a dismissive hand at the pile of prospectuses. "A man of the measure. Son, why didn't you just say so? Forget this folderol; let's talk real business."
