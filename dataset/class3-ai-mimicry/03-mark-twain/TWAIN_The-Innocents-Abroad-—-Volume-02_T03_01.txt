My Uncle Bartholomew, freshly returned from his grand tour of Europe’s finer ruins, held the dinner table hostage with a long and tearful account of a certain Duke’s crumbling chateau. He spoke of its noble lineage, its tragic disrepair, its hallowed halls where precisely nothing of consequence had occurred for two centuries—and he spoke of it all as if it were the very heart of civilization. A profound sadness seized me then, a sorrow so deep it felt like drowning in dust. I looked at his trembling hands, gesturing toward some imagined parapet in the air, and then at my father’s hands, hard and stained from the practical work of building a town. We were a people who harnessed rivers and laid down steel, and here was my own kin weeping for a pile of stones whose chief virtue was its magnificent, titled uselessness. It was a worship of ghosts, a romance with decay, and I felt a sudden, desperate urge to throw open a window and let in some honest, uncomplicated American air.
