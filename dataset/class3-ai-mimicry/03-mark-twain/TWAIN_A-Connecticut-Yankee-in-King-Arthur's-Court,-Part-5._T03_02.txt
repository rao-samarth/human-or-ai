The lamp flickered, throwing long, dancing shadows that made Deacon Albright look a sight more formidable than he had any right to be. He sat across my kitchen table, his belly a proud declaration of the Lord’s bounty, and spoke in a low, righteous hum about the absolute necessity of new velvet for the collection plates. It was a matter of dignity, he explained, a way to properly honor the coin offered up to the Almighty. All the while, my eyes kept drifting to the small, knotted cloth on the mantelpiece, which held what little I’d set aside for seed and a new pair of boots for the boy. The Deacon claimed a man’s soul was polished by such sacrifice, but it seemed to me a peculiar sort of polishing, one that left a fellow’s child barefoot and his fields fallow. He was building a grand mansion in the hereafter, he said, using my bricks and mortar; a proposition that gave me the cold certainty I’d be spending eternity sleeping in the yard.
