He jabbed a finger toward the colossal archway, a thing of such hoary and solemn magnificence you’d think a procession of Caesars was due any minute. “Can you not feel it?” he demanded, his eyes wide with a fool’s sincerity. “The weight of ages! The echoes of empire!” A fine sentiment, I’m sure, if a man wasn’t presently trying to sell me a brace of questionable pigeons right out from under my nose, and if the air itself wasn’t thick enough to chew—a potent stew of roasting nuts, cheap perfume, and something unholy simmering in a nearby gutter. “You have no soul for poetry!” he hissed, recoiling as a child smeared something sticky on his trousers. “Perhaps not,” I shot back, dodging a sputtering motor-cart that belched a cloud of pure villainy in our direction. “But I have a very keen soul for avoiding cholera, and I’ll wager this glorious monument has seen more of that in the last week than it has seen genuine nobility in a thousand years!”
