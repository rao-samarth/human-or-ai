I stood there in that great press of humanity, where the avenues narrowed and the old stones sweated under the noon sun, and I watched them barter for their little bits of memory. A man with a voice like a rusty hinge was hollering the virtues of his miniature cathedrals carved from soap, while another sold thimbles said to be blessed by a ghost who’d long since given up the business of haunting. Every miracle had its price tag; every hallowed story was condensed to fit on the back of a postcard sold for a copper or two. There was no room for genuine reverence in the crush of it all—it had been elbowed out, you see, by the much more pressing and profitable enterprise of peddling sanctified trinkets to folks who wished to buy a feeling they could not quite find on their own. The whole affair left a lonesome ache in the soul, a quiet certainty that the original article, whatever it might have been, was now buried too deep under the marketplace to ever be found again.
