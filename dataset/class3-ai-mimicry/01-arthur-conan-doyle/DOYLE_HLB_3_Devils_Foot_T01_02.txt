You see that crumbling manor upon the hill, my dear fellow? It stands as a grim monument to the folly of the Penhaligon brothers. I knew them in their youth, when a shared crust of bread was a thing for laughter and camaraderie. Yet the discovery of their uncle’s immense fortune acted not as a balm, but as a most potent acid upon the bonds of kinship. What began as quiet legal wrangling in dusty solicitors’ offices soon erupted into public accusations of the vilest sort—each brother seeking to beggar the other, to strip him of name and honour for the sake of a few more acres, a handful more sovereigns. In the end, they succeeded only in destroying their inheritance and themselves, leaving that hollowed-out shell of a house as the sole headstone for a family consumed entirely by its own insatiable appetite for gold.
