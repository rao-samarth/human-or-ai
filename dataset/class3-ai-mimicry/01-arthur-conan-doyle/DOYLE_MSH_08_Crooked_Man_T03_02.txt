It was in the hushed quiet of the Governor’s library, amidst the scent of old leather and port, that I saw Sir Alistair’s composure finally shatter. He had borne the evening’s festivities with a strained, almost feverish gaiety, but the late arrival of a certain clergyman from the shires had visibly undone him. The cleric, a mild man with unassuming eyes, cornered him by the globe. “Sir Alistair,” he murmured, his voice barely disturbing the air, “forgive an old man’s memory, but was it not in the autumn of ’78 that your younger brother’s commission was so suddenly rescinded? A most unfortunate affair in the Khyber Pass, I seem to recall.” A ghastly pallor spread across Sir Alistair’s countenance. He gave a single, strangled gasp, his hand flying to his heart as if to physically restrain a truth that had been caged there for thirty years. He swayed, his eyes wide with a terror that no living man could inspire, and collapsed amongst the damask chairs—a man struck down not by apoplexy, but by the sudden, fatal recollection
