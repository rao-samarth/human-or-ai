My dear Edward,” my uncle began, swirling the last of the brandy in his glass as the distant sound of carols drifted from the parlour, “what you propose is not the cold, impartial machinery of the law, but rather the hot-blooded strike of personal retribution. It is a most natural impulse, I grant you, one which many a man has felt; yet it is an impulse which civilisation, for its own preservation, has rightly sought to tame. To see a rogue brought to account is one thing—a matter for the courts, for evidence weighed and a sentence delivered with dispassionate clarity. But to desire that the villain suffer in a manner identical to his victim, to wish to be the very instrument of that suffering—that, I fear, is to descend to his own lamentable level. The one serves the whole of mankind by upholding a principle; the other serves only the raw, singular satisfaction of a wounded spirit, a satisfaction which, once tasted, often leaves a most bitter aftertaste indeed.
