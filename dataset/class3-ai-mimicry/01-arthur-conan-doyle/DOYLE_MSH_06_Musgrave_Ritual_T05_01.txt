The entire machinery of Mr Alistair Finch’s public ruination was set in motion not by some vulgar act of passion, but by the quiet, methodical hand of a woman he had so carelessly discarded. She had spent the preceding months gathering the disparate threads of his various indiscretions—his speculative follies in the South American markets, his clandestine dealings with disreputable associates, the very letters he had penned to her in moments of misguided confidence. Now, as Finch held court in the most exclusive dining club off Fleet Street, laughing with men whose fortunes were inextricably tied to his own, he remained utterly ignorant of the storm about to break. A single, carefully worded dispatch, sent an hour prior to the editor of a prominent gazette, contained every damning particular. His financial house, his social standing, his very name—all were built upon a foundation of sand, and the tide, set in motion by a most formidable and calculated fury, was even now beginning to turn.
