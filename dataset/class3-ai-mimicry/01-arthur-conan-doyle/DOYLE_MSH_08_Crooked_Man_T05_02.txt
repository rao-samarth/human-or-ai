I stood by the window, the damning page from the accounts clutched in my hand, its neat columns a stark monument to a meticulous fraud. Across the hushed office, young Finlay sat hunched over his own work, the very picture of diligent servitude; his brow was furrowed in concentration, his pen scratched with a steady, almost pious rhythm. To any casual observer, he was the model clerk, the foundation upon which our firm's sterling reputation was built. And yet, I knew the truth of it—that this veneer of absolute probity was a mask for a desperate man, and that the coins he pilfered were destined not for some wastrel’s indulgence but, as I had strong reason to suspect, for a sick wife and a mountain of unavoidable debt. My duty was clear, a straight and simple path. To expose him was to uphold the honour of the establishment, yet to do so felt less like an act of justice and more like the grim business of an executioner.
