It was upon the old stone stile, half-overgrown with ivy at the edge of the copse, that I first perceived the curious markings. They were not the idle scratchings of a passing rustic, nor were they the work of nature, for their precision and peculiar repetition bespoke a definite, if inscrutable, purpose. A small circle, followed by a pair of wavy lines and then a simple cross—what manner of intelligence sought to convey its meaning through such rudimentary figures? One might at first surmise it to be a tramp’s code, a primitive system by which vagrants pass intelligence concerning the disposition of the local households. Yet the deliberate placement and the very freshness of the incisions suggested a mind of a more calculating order. Here was a problem of a most singular nature, a silent dialogue etched in granite, awaiting the observer who possessed the wit not merely to see the characters, but to comprehend the alphabet in which they were written.
