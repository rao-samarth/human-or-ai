Lord Ashworth leaned forward, his teacup rattling in its saucer, a most undignified display for a man whose treatises on logic were required reading at the university. "My dear Mrs Gable," he began, his voice straining for a tone of patient instruction, "you have stumbled upon a fortunate coincidence, a mere anomaly. You cannot possibly grasp the intricate web of causality I have spent a decade constructing." He gestured vaguely at a pile of scattered papers, as if their sheer volume were proof of his genius. She, however, did not even glance at them. She simply adjusted the pin in her hat, her expression one of polite, almost clinical, disinterest. "It is no web, my lord," she stated, her words as precise and cold as a surgeon's scalpel. "It is a single, frayed thread, and you have been tugging at the wrong end from the start." The silence that followed was profound, broken only by the man’s sharp intake of breath—the sound of an intellect so vast and celebrated being deflated by a pinprick of simple, unassailable fact.
