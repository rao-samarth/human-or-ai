It was a scene of the most lamentable chaos, precipitated not by some grand political quarrel, but by the simple discovery of a sliver of silver lode on the old common land. The matter began with shouted words in the public house, yet it spilled into the cobbled square with the fury of a burst dam. I saw old Hemlock, the surveyor, clutching a bloodied map to his chest whilst the blacksmith’s two sons laid into him with brutish fists, their faces contorted with a grasping rage. A moment later, the baker’s cart was overturned, and a shot rang out from an upper window, shattering a pane of glass in the apothecary opposite. The entire town, it seemed, had been seized by a violent madness, each man seeing in his neighbour an obstacle to a fortune he did not yet possess. Here was the base nature of man laid bare; a frantic, destructive scramble for a prize which, in the end, would be stained by the blood of its pursuers.
