As I sat upon this rustic stile, my gaze sweeping across the placid, sun-drenched downs, my thoughts turned nonetheless to the grim affair at the old grange. It is a singular and dreadful thing, I reflected, to witness a household turn upon itself; a more malignant affliction than any fever which a physician might be called upon to diagnose. No external foe, no matter how cunning, can inflict a wound so deep or so persistent as that which is dealt by one’s own blood about the dinner table. The poison, once introduced through whispers and resentments, does not merely sicken the individual but seems to permeate the very timbers of the house, souring the air and leaving a stain upon all that was once wholesome. The most astute mind may untangle a criminal’s plot, but what intellect can ever hope to mend the fractures of a broken hearth, or restore the simple faith that has been so utterly and irrevocably shattered?
