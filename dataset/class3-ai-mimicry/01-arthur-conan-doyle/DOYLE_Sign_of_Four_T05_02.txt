In a parish so small as ours, where every soul is known to the other, the burden carried by the Ainsworth family became a thing of public, though unspoken, conjecture. The matter of the youngest daughter, who had been sent away so abruptly under the guise of a continental education, was a wound that refused to close. One could perceive its poison in the very air of their cottage—in the mother’s perpetually startled expression, in the father’s sudden and violent tempers which flared over the most trifling of matters. This concealed tragedy did not remain inert; rather, it became an active agent of decay, breeding suspicion between husband and wife and transforming their once-warm hearth into a cold monument to what could not be said. It was a ghastly spectacle, demonstrating how a single, guarded falsehood can fester until it has corrupted the entire structure of a household, leaving naught but a hollowed-out shell of affection.
