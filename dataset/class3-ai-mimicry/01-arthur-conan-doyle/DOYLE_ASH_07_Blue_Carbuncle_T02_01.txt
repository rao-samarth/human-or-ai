The press of humanity upon the pavement was a torrent, a ceaseless river of faces in which individual character seemed utterly submerged. Yet one countenance held my gaze—that of a man of some sixty years, whose sober dress and placid, benevolent features bespoke a life of quiet charity, perhaps a country vicar lost amidst the city’s roar. He carried a small, leather-bound book of psalms, his thumb marking a page. One might have passed him by a thousand times and thought him the very model of gentle piety. But as a wretched street-arab stumbled and fell before him, I saw it—not a flicker of pity, not a moment’s concern, but a flash of cold, reptilian contempt in those pale eyes, a tightening of the lips so cruel and so profound that it betrayed in an instant the sanctimonious shell. In that single, unguarded second, the carefully constructed facade of a lifetime crumbled, revealing the appalling chasm which lay between the man he appeared to be and the monster he undoubtedly was.
