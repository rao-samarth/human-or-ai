I have often reflected that the passions of a man in a small community burn with a peculiar and dangerous intensity. In the great city, a fellow's ardour is but one small flame amongst a million, easily lost and soon forgotten; yet here, in the compressed atmosphere of our parish, it becomes a veritable furnace. We all observed the young solicitor’s courtship, an affair whose devotion to the draper’s daughter was at first a thing of local admiration, spoken of with smiles over tea. But this general approval slowly gave way to a certain public unease, as his sentiment, which had once manifested in flowers and poetry, festered into a brooding, unreasoning jealousy. The end of the matter was as swift as it was dreadful—a final, violent silencing of the very object he had claimed to worship, proving with grim finality that the heart which loves with the most desperate fire is often the very one which leaves behind naught but the coldest cinders
