Mr. Alistair Finch found little solace upon the rustic bench, though the chalk stream before him burbled with a peace entirely foreign to his own agitated spirit. To the casual eye, he was merely a gentleman of means, taking the country air; yet this was a fa√ßade, a carefully constructed artifice behind which a quite different man resided in a state of perpetual alarm. Every distant cry of a rook, every rustle in the hedgerow, seemed to his overwrought senses the imminent footfall of discovery. The sheer, grinding weight of the deception was a thing that now followed him into his dreams, robbing him of rest and etching new, deeper lines about his weary eyes. He pressed a hand to his brow, not against the sun, but against the ceaseless, wearing necessity of being two men at once, and he wondered, with a cold dread, which of them would be left when the entire wretched structure inevitably came crashing down.
