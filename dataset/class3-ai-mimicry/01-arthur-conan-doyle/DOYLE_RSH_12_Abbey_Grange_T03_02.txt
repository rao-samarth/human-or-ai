The General stood by the fire, his shadow immense and flickering upon the wall, his gaze fixed not upon his anxious solicitor but upon some distant, inner point of principle. He had been warned, most emphatically, of the consequences—of the public disgrace, the financial ruin, and the veritable end of a storied career which would surely follow his proposed course of action. Yet he would not be dissuaded. There was, in his mind, an incontrovertible logic to this self-immolation; for a lady’s honour had been publicly impugned by a scoundrel, and though she had explicitly begged him to let the matter rest, his own code compelled him to act. It was a duty, as he saw it, that superseded all private considerations of comfort or safety. To him, this was not a matter of choice but of moral necessity, the final and absolute obligation of a gentleman to defend the defenceless, even if the instrument of that defence was his own destruction.
