The Inspector adjusted his spectacles, his gaze fixed not upon his agitated companion but upon some distant point in the shadowed room, a point where all the threads of his long inquiry seemed to converge. “You must understand,” he began, his voice devoid of the younger man’s frantic energy, “that these fifteen years have not been a period of neglect, but rather one of exceedingly careful observation. Our quarry, Mr Davies, has grown comfortable, you see; he has come to believe his past transgression is buried as deeply as its victim. This perceived security, this hitherto unshakable confidence, has been his undoing. He has made a small, yet fatal, error—a single boast in a letter to an associate abroad, a letter which I now hold.” He tapped a crisp envelope upon the mahogany desk, and the sound, slight as it was, seemed to the younger man to be the very fall of a guillotine. The time for waiting was, at long last, entirely at an end.
