The men at the adjacent table speak loudly of their commercial misfortunes, tallying their losses as a shopkeeper might his spoiled wares. They believe they comprehend disaster, yet their understanding is as shallow as the dregs in their glasses. What, after all, is a depleted coffer when set against the utter insolvency of a man’s confidence? When an enterprise to which one has dedicated his entire intellect—his every waking thought and faculty—is brought to ruin, the true sting is not a financial one. It is, rather, the calamitous realisation that his own judgement, that most trusted and finely-honed instrument, has proven profoundly defective. The public may see a simple failed venture, a matter for the courts and creditors; they remain entirely blind to the true wreckage, the quiet and catastrophic demolition of a man's belief in the very powers that once defined him.
