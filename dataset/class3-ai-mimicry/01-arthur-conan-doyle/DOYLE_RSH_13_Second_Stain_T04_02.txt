We had taken a bench in the public gardens, the clamour of the city a distant hum, when my companion gestured with his cane towards a passing constable. “Observe that man, Davies,” he began, his voice low. “His very bearing speaks of a duty which he believes to be righteous and absolute; yet, how often must he, in the execution of that duty, withhold some small, yet vital, fact from a grieving widow or an anxious father?” I confessed I had never considered the matter in such a light. “Indeed,” he continued, tapping his cane softly upon the gravel. “There are truths so corrosive, so utterly destructive to the fragile peace of a household, that their full utterance would constitute a far greater crime than their careful suppression. Tell me, is the physician who offers a gentle fiction in place of a brutal finality to be condemned as a liar? Or is he, rather, to be commended as a preserver of that most precious and fleeting of commodities: a man’s final days of hope?” A woman nearby, adjusting her hat against the breeze, glanced over, her expression unreadable before she turned away.
