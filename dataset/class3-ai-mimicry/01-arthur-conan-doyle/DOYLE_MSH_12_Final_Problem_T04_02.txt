The Viscount sat quite still, his long fingers steepled before him, and regarded the man who paced before the hearth with an expression of mild, almost academic, interest. Every avenue of escape, every conceivable line of retreat, had been systematically closed to his guest, who now found himself hemmed in not by walls of brick and mortar, but by a far more unyielding prison of irrefutable logic. The perspiration which beaded upon the man’s brow was not born of the fire’s heat, but of a cold and creeping dread; he had played a most desperate game and had lost, and now the time had come to pay the reckoning. There was no appeal to be made, no mercy to be sought from the cold grey eyes which followed his every movement. Upon the mantelpiece, between a tarnished silver cup and a row of chemical treatises, lay a small, blue-glass phial, and it was towards this object that both men’s eyes were inexorably drawn, for in its shadowed contents lay the sole and terrible solution which remained.
