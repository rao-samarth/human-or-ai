I watched from my armchair as my cousin Arthur, a man of ordinarily sound reason, calmly claimed responsibility for the disastrous rumour that had nearly undone the Clavering engagement. He did so before the assembled family, knowing full well the whispers and censure that would follow him for years; yet I knew, with the certainty of a proven theorem, that the true source of the slander was his younger sister, whose own fragile standing could not have withstood the gale of such a revelation. There is, I reflected, a peculiar and lonely form of valour which consists not in the bold charge or the public defence, but in the quiet acceptance of a stain upon one’s own character for the preservation of another’s. He had deliberately donned the cloak of a scoundrel to ensure his sister might still wear the spotless gown of an innocent, a silent and unlauded transaction of virtue for which the world offers no medals, only the cold shoulder of its disdain.
