Observe, my dear fellow,” he murmured, his voice barely rising above the general hum of the square, “the curious little drama which unfolds before us. You see that portly gentleman by the fountain, the one with the unfortunate taste in neckties? One might suppose him to be quite alone, yet I should venture he is the most thoroughly guarded man in all of London. Mark the flower-girl to his left; she has not sold a single rose in ten minutes, though her gaze never strays far from the path. Note also the cabman across the way, whose horse has been patiently waiting for a fare that never materialises, and the urchin pretending to polish a boot-heel with a singular lack of industry. Each is a fixed point, a sentinel in disguise, forming an invisible cordon of which our friend in the cravat is the oblivious centre. A most fascinating, and I daresay, a most effective arrangement for ensuring a man’s continued existence.
