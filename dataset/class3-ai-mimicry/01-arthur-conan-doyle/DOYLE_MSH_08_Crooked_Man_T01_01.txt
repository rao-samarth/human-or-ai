I hold it as a firm principle that the human mind is a master of self-deception, and never more so than when confronted by an impossibility. It was, therefore, with a determined logic that I dismissed the fellow I saw across the omnibus-choked thoroughfare. A mere trick of the flickering gaslight upon a stranger’s features, I told myself; a common enough face, after all, in this great and anonymous city. And yet, for all my rationalisation, the image persisted with a most unwelcome clarity. The curious tilt of the hat, the particular manner in which he clutched his walking stick—these were the unmistakable idiosyncrasies of a man whom I had every reason to believe had met his end in the colonies some five years ago. I hurried on, my heart a frantic drum against my ribs, for I knew, with a certainty that defied all reason, that a debt I had long considered settled was about to be presented for collection.
