In the quiet parish of Oakhaven, where a man’s reputation was as solid a currency as the minted sovereign, Mr. Elias Thorne had for a full score of years been a veritable pillar of the community. Yet it is a primary law of the universe, as certain as gravity itself, that a deed once done cannot be undone, and its ripples, however faint, must eventually reach the shore. The affair of the missing funds from his first clerkship in the city—a youthful indiscretion born of desperation and swiftly buried—had seemed a spectre long since laid to rest. But such phantoms are patient things, and the recent arrival of a certain keen-faced gentleman at the local inn was the first material sign of its return. Mr. Thorne could feel the carefully constructed edifice of his later life begin to shift upon its foundations, for he understood, with a cold and dreadful clarity, that an old debt had at last come due, and its collection could no longer be postponed
