The carriage wheels did groan upon the rutted path, a doleful music for her heaven-bent thoughts. He, my lord, spoke of naught but the northern shires and how their fealty, once sworn, would gird his claim with such a strength as to make a king himself take notice. He saw in every league a forward step toward some gilded throne, in every passing wood a source of timber for his banners and his war-host. But she, good soul, saw only the dust of mortal striving, the fleeting vanity of earthly crowns, and her eyes, fixed not upon the map he held but on the little cross that hung about her neck, did well with tears for his soul’s great peril. “My lord,” she whispered, her voice a fragile prayer against the thunder of his will, “what gain is a province if our immortal part be forfeit? This road leads us not to glory, but perchance to judgement.” He did but scoff and turn again to his charts, his heart a map where God’s own kingdom was not marked.
