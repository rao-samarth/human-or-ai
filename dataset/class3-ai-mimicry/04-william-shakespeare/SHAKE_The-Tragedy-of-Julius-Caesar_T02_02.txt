Mark him well, my friend,” the old merchant grumbled, gesturing with a trembling hand towards the magistrate’s passing litter. “See how he doth swell with office, his very garments straining with the pride that devours him from within. Once he was a man of simple means and honest speech, yet he coveted a perch far above his station; and in his hungry climb, he shed his conscience as a serpent sheds its skin.” The younger man beside him merely smiled, his eyes glinting with a sharp, familiar light. “And yet, he climbs still,” he countered softly, the city’s clamour nearly swallowing his words. “Hath his conscience bought him bread, or his virtue a roof against the rain? Nay. I say let a man’s soul be the coin he spends for greatness, for what is a spirit worth that cannot purchase a world? Better to be a sated king in hell than a starving saint in heaven’s gutter.
