Amidst the gilded hall, where flatterers did prate with sugared tongues and pour sweet poison into the Duke’s unwary ear, Lord Cassian stood a stone of silent discontent. His was a countenance unpractised in the fawning arts, a mien that bespoke a soul at war with its own circumstance. He had been bid to lend his good name to a venture most foul, a decree that would enrich the coffers of the powerful whilst grinding the poor to dust; and for this service, a promise of high station whispered in the shadows. To what end serves a man’s honour, he did ponder, if it be but a coin to be spent for worldly gain? The wine in his goblet seemed turned to gall, and the music a shrill and mocking dirge, for his conscience, that unbribable judge, had already passed its heavy sentence upon the base proposal. He saw the gilded path of advancement, yet knew its toll was the very marrow of his own soul.
