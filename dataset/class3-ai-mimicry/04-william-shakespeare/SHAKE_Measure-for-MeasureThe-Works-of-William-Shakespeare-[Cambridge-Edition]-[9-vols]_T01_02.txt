Lord Bramwell sat in judgment, his brow a furrowed field of vexation, whilst before him stood the cause: a trembling page who had, with damnable audacity, purloined the final marzipan swan. The law, as barked by the puffed-chested steward, demanded a public whipping for such an affront to the evening's dignity, a stern and righteous balancing of the scales. Yet Her Ladyship, whose heart was ever a softer substance than her husband's head, did plead for clemency, arguing that the boy's stomach, and not his soul, was the true villain of the piece. To punish a rumbling belly as one would a traitorous heart seemed a disproportionate cruelty, she declared, for what is a confection against the tears of a child?
