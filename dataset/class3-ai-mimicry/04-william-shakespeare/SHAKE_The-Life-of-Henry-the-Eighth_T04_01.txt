The Bursar struck the heavy ledger upon his oaken desk, its pages writ with promises he had no mind to keep. “Hark, mistress,” he commanded, his voice a low and urgent thunder, “see these sums are sent to the alderman’s coffer ere the evening bell doth toll. We purchase his silence, and with that, the whole council’s favour; a prize of far more substance than the pittance vowed to the alms-house.” His clerk, a woman of quiet countenance whose thoughts were more on heaven than on earth, let fall her quill. “My lord,” she answered, her voice a fragile plea, “that coin was consecrated to God’s own work, a shield for the wretched against winter’s cruel breath. To turn it so, for to buy a statesman’s fleeting whisper, is to mock the very saints we pray to.
