He stood beneath the boughs of that most ancient oak, his knuckles grown as white as bone upon his staff, and watched her. What was it? A simple smile she did bestow upon the cooper’s son, a kindness merely for a mended pail, yet to his fever’d mind, a treasonous pact was sealed in the full light of day. With a cry that startled the grazing sheep, he strode forth upon the green, his voice a ragged thunder for all the parish folk to mark. “Fie, madam!” he proclaimed, his finger a shaking spear. “Dost thou so cheaply trade thy vows for a flattering tongue and a rogue’s glance?” The poor maid’s face, a moment past a rose of summer, turned to winter’s frost, whilst the boy stared with mouth agape, the pail clattering from his grasp. A hush fell, broken only by a titter from the baker’s wife, for all there knew the lad was but her cousin. And thus the man, seeing not his error but only the silent judgment in their eyes, stood marooned in his own mad choler, his honour not defended but drowned in the very poison he himself had brewed.
