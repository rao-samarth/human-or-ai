Lena slid the final report across the desk, its stark red numbers facing up. “I tried to warn you, Marcus. Weeks ago.” Her voice was quiet, not accusatory, which somehow made it worse. Marcus stared at the page, but he wasn’t reading it. He was replaying the board meeting, the way he’d swaggered in, promising revolutionary results, dismissing every potential pitfall with a wave of his hand. He’d practically laughed when Lena brought up the data discrepancies. “A minor hiccup,” he’d called it. Now, the silence in his office felt suffocating, thick with the phantom echoes of his own boasts. He could still feel the weight of their gazes on him as the system crashed live, the smug certainty draining from his face like blood. He finally looked up at her, the bravado gone, replaced by something hollow. “I know,” he said, the words barely a whisper. “I know you did.” The admission cost him everything.
