The city’s planners had envisioned the grand plaza as a perfect conduit, a channel for the orderly flow of citizens from the transit hubs to the administrative towers. Every signal, every holoscreen, every subtle incline in the pavement was engineered to guide and suggest, to make compliance the path of least resistance. Yet, the system's greatest vulnerability was never a coordinated uprising, but the simple, unpredictable friction of a single human will. It was the woman who stopped mid-stride to watch a kestrel circling a skyscraper’s peak, disrupting the hurried current behind her. It was the courier who took the long way through the old market, for no reason other than the scent of baked bread. These were not acts of overt protest; they were assertions of existence. Each deviation, however small, introduced a quantum of chaos into the predictable machine, a quiet reminder that a map is not the territory, and a person is not a particle.
