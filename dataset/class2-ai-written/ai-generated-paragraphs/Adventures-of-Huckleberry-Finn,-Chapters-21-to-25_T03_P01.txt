He stood near the center of the reception hall, a study in calculated humility. When he spoke, his voice was pitched just low enough to draw people in, forcing them to lean closer. He employed a self-deprecating anecdote about a recent, well-publicized failure, and the surrounding laughter was both immediate and a little too forceful. Every movement was curated: the open-palmed gestures that signaled transparency, the way his gaze held on one person for a beat too long to convey profound interest. This wasn't a conversation; it was a broadcast of sincerity. It was a subtle, masterful production designed not to communicate genuine feeling, but to manage it in others. He wasn't connecting with people; he was directing a scene, and his carefully constructed vulnerability was his most effective tool.
