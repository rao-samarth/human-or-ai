The clatter of a fork hitting a plate was the only sound that broke the suffocating silence around the dining table. I looked from my father’s stony expression to my aunt, who was suddenly fascinated by the pattern on her napkin. “You’ve known me my whole life,” I said, my voice shaking at first, then hardening with a desperate clarity. “You taught me to tell the truth, to be kind. You saw every scraped knee, every report card, every clumsy, earnest attempt to be a good person. And now you sit here, letting this… this lie hang in the air between us as if it has more weight than twenty years of my life?” I pushed my chair back, the legs scraping against the floor. “I can’t show you a receipt for my integrity. I can’t produce a signed affidavit of my own character. All I have is me. All I have is the person you raised. Is that not enough anymore?”
