“You have to admit, that was something special,” he said, turning the small, molded plastic replica over in his hands as the bus pulled away from the valley. She didn't answer, just kept her eyes fixed on the blur of the ancient landscape through the tinted window. He tried again. “It makes you think, doesn’t it?” That got a reaction. “It makes me think about how easily you can build a snack bar and a gift shop on a mass grave,” she said, her voice low but sharp. “That’s not fair. The money goes to preservation, to keeping the site open for people like us.” He sounded defensive. “For people like us to buy cheap souvenirs and listen to an audio guide that sounds like a car commercial? They’ve packaged a tragedy. They've turned an epoch into an afternoon excursion with an admission fee, and you bought the keychain.”
