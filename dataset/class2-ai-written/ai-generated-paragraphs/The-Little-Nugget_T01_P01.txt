His finger traced a jagged line across the topographical map, the single desk lamp casting his face in sharp, eager shadows. “It’s foolproof, I tell you.” His voice was a low, urgent hum. “We acquire the parcel for next to nothing. Then, for three, maybe four nights, the trucks come in from the old quarry. No one uses that road. They dump the kaolin, we till it into the topsoil before dawn, and then we innocently commission a geological survey.” He leaned back, a triumphant gleam in his eyes, as if he could already feel the money. “The surveyor finds a 'miraculous' deposit, the ceramics conglomerate gets wind of it, and our useless patch of dirt becomes a goldmine.” He paused, waiting for my applause. All I could picture was a convoy of rumbling trucks getting stuck in the mud under a full moon, their headlights a beacon for any curious neighbor for miles.
