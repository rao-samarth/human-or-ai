From his position by the grill, he watched her laugh, a sound he once loved but which now, directed at her old university friend, grated like metal on stone. A hot, familiar acid churned in his stomach. The friend’s hand briefly touched her elbow to emphasize a point, a gesture so casual it should have been invisible, but to him, it was a searing brand. He set his tongs down with a clatter that cut through the garden chatter. Striding across the lawn, he wedged himself between them, his arm snaking around her waist with a proprietary tightness. “Hope you’re not sharing all our secrets,” he said, his smile a brittle, unconvincing mask. A sudden hush fell over the nearby clusters of relatives. He saw it all in a sickening rush: her bright expression hardening into disbelief, the pity in his uncle’s eyes, the abrupt interest his mother-in-law took in her plate of potato salad. The afternoon sun felt accusatory on his neck; he had made the monster in his head visible to everyone.
