He watched them from the other side of the glass, the hurried silhouettes against the rain-slicked street. In his worn uniform, he was just part of the building's architecture, another shadow holding a mop. The people outside, with their sharp coats and purposeful strides, seemed to inhabit a different reality, one where the future was a clean, well-lit corridor. He wondered if they ever thought about the intricate patterns of condensation on a window, or the quiet symphony of a city settling after midnight. He once read about how every person carries an entire, unwritten novel inside them. Looking at the reflections—his own tired face superimposed over a businessman hailing a cab—he suspected most of those stories were not about success, but about the quiet ache of what was almost said, or the beautiful things noticed when no one else was looking.
