The siren that wailed past their fourth-floor window at 2 a.m. was just another part of the city’s irregular heartbeat, a sound they had learned to sleep through. In the six months since they’d moved in, the external noise had become a strange sort of constant, a backdrop against which they were figuring out their own, much quieter, rhythm. It was in the small, unspoken agreements: who made the coffee while the other cleared the tiny counter, the shared look across a crowded subway car that said "I'm ready to go home," the way they could sit in silence for an hour, backs against opposite ends of the sofa, each absorbed in a book, yet feeling entirely connected. These weren't the grand milestones they had imagined, but rather the subtle mechanics of a shared life being assembled piece by piece, a quiet construction project taking place amidst the relentless clamor of the world outside.
