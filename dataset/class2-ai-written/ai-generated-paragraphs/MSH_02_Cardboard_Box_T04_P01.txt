The applause felt like a wave of static, and the champagne had long since turned sour in my mouth. It wasn’t jealousy, you have to understand. It was a point of clarification. He raised his glass, calling her his whole world, and the lie was just too loud, too obscene to let stand in this room of polite smiles. The path to the cake table was surprisingly clear. I remember thinking how silly the ornate little server looked, all silver filigree for something as soft as cake. It felt heavier than I expected. I only meant to interrupt the speech, to make a statement, but the arc it made through the air was so clean, so right. The way his toast ended in a sort of wet cough, the surprise in his eyes—that was the first real thing I’d seen all night. A single, perfect crimson flower bloomed on his starched white shirt, finally adding some genuine color to the evening. Now everyone could see. Now the picture was correct.
