The low hum of the ballroom suddenly felt like a physical pressure against my ears. Then I saw him, holding a flute of champagne, deep in conversation with my director. A cold dread, sharp and immediate, seized my chest. It wasn’t just him; it was the entire flimsy reality I had constructed for his benefit now standing just twenty feet away, threatening to shatter like cheap glass. All those casual, inflated claims I made at the conference—stupid, pointless boasts meant to impress a stranger—were about to boomerang with devastating accuracy. The starched collar of my shirt felt like a noose. Every smiling face seemed to be watching, waiting for the moment of my unraveling. There was no air left in the room, only the suffocating scent of lilies and regret. I had to move. Slipping back through the catered smiles and the clinking crystal, I searched for any exit, any anonymous hallway that would swallow me whole before my own foolish words could.
