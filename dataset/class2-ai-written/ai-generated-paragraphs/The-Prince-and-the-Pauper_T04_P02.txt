He cornered her near the terrace doors, the polite murmur of the orchestra a faint backdrop to his hiss. “You have no right to stand there, wearing that seal,” he said, his eyes fixed on the insignia pinned to her simple, dark suit. “My family bled for that crest. It is mine by blood, by a thousand years of history.” She met his gaze without flinching, her own voice calm and impossibly clear in the opulent hall. “Your family’s history is written in ink and stone. Mine is being written today, in the streets, by people who have never seen the inside of a hall like this.” He scoffed, a bitter, ugly sound. “They’re commoners. What authority can they give you?” She gave the barest hint of a smile, a slight, dangerous curving of her lips. “The only kind that matters. The kind you can’t inherit.”
