The midday crowd surged past the bus shelter, a current of shoulders and shopping bags that parted around her as if she were a rock. Horns blared a familiar, impatient rhythm while the smell of fried onions from a street cart mingled with the damp scent of pavement. She watched the faces, a blur of strangers, and wondered how many of them would believe it if they knew. The story was out there now, a fiction that had grown more solid than her own memory of the truth. It was a brand on her name. A bus pulled up, its brakes sighing, and for a moment she saw a distorted version of herself in the grimy window. The eyes that stared back looked haunted, older. They belonged to the person in their story, not to her. The doors hissed open, an invitation to somewhere else, but her feet felt bolted to the ground, anchored by the weight of a life that was no longer hers.
