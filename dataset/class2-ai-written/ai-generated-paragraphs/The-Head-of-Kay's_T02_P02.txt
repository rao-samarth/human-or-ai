The dean droned on from the podium, his voice a smooth, practiced monotone that filled the cavernous hall. He spoke of legacy and responsibility, of a future shaped by the very traditions the students in their stiff collars and polished shoes were currently enduring. In the third row, a girl meticulously folded her program into a sharp-edged bird, its paper wings a silent, intricate protest. A few seats down, a boy met his friend's gaze, a flicker of shared amusement passing between them before they both dutifully returned their attention forward. They were all playing their parts, a sea of polite nods and feigned interest. But beneath the surface, a different current was moving. It was in the restless shifting of feet, the almost imperceptible sighs, and the collective, unspoken wish for the clock on the back wall to just move a little faster. The words from the stage were meant to inspire them, but they felt more like a cage.
