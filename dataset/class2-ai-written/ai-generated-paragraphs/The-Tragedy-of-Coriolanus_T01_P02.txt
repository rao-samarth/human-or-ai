The engine coughed once, then died. A profound silence rushed in to fill the space where the steady hum had been, broken only by the ticking of cooling metal. He’d laughed at the faded, hand-painted sign miles back: “Last Gas 100 Miles.” A warning for tourists, for the unprepared, not for him. He had checked the map, calculated the distance, and decided his knowledge of the vehicle and the terrain superseded a piece of rotting wood. Now, the needle rested firmly on empty, an unblinking accuser. He gripped the steering wheel, the polished leather suddenly cold and foreign. Outside, the desert stretched into a shimmering, indifferent horizon. There was no one coming. The only thing left to do was sit here and confront the sheer, stupid weight of his own certainty.
