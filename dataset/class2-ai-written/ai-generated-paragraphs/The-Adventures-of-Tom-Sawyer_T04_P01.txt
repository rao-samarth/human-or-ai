The only light came from the small lamp beside his chair, catching the amber of the whiskey he swirled in his glass. Ice clinked softly. Just a few hours ago, at the historical society fundraiser, he’d been holding court by the punch bowl, his voice low but carrying as he lamented the state of the town’s youth. He’d shaken his head with such theatrical sorrow over the Peterson boy being caught with a flask behind the bleachers last week. A complete lack of guidance, he’d declared to anyone who would listen. A failure of character. Now, he drained his third glass, the burn of the alcohol a private, respectable comfort. His was a civilized drink for a man of standing, of course; nothing at all to do with the cheap liquor of a troubled teenager. His was different, because no one was here to see it.
