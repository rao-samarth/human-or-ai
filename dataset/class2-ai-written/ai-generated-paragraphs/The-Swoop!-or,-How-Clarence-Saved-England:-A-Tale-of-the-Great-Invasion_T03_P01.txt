The last fleck of silver paint was drying on the miniature wheelbarrow, and he was already late. Through the window, the lights of the community hall blazed, a beacon for the championship everyone had been preparing for all year. But his phone was still warm in his hand from the panicked call. Old Mrs. Albright’s daughter, two states away, her voice tight with a worry that felt far more real than any painted figurine. Her mother wasn't answering, and her house was dark on the other side of town. Going there would mean missing everything—the judging, the hushed anticipation, the shared, ridiculous glory of it all. He glanced from the shimmering, absurdly perfect scene on his workbench to the car keys hanging by the door. The distant thrum of celebration pulled him one way, while a deep, unsettling quiet from across the darkened fields pulled him in another. He had to choose, and he had to choose now.
