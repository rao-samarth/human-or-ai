The low murmur of conversation and the clinking of champagne flutes filled the ballroom, a sound meant to signify celebration. I watched the reflections in the polished floor, distorted figures gliding through a world of light and ease. It struck me then, not as a sudden revelation but as a slow, creeping certainty, that I was merely wearing the costume of someone my age. The suit felt borrowed, the smile a careful performance. Inside, a different clock was ticking, one calibrated not to youthful whims but to the immense gravity of duties assumed too soon. Others here could afford to dream in hypotheticals, to map out lives with branching paths of pure potential. My own map had been drawn long ago, a single, unalterable line heading straight toward a horizon I was responsible for building, stone by heavy stone. The weight of it didn't feel heroic; it simply felt like an absence, the quiet, hollow space where a lighter existence was supposed to be.
