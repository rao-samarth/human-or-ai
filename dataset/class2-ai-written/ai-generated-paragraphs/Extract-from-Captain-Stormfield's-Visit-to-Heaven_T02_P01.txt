My thumb hovered over the call button, the screen’s light a small, anxious beacon in the twilight. Pushing it would mean the end of the old bakery, the end of Sarah’s life’s work, all for the sake of a zoning bylaw written decades ago. The whole town was waiting, it seemed, holding its breath to see what I would do. My choice felt like a fulcrum on which futures would turn, a decision of immense local gravity. But then a cool breeze stirred the pines, and I tilted my head back. Up there, past the orange glow of our streetlights, the first stars were emerging. A satellite, a silent pinprick of human ambition, drifted across an ocean of indifferent constellations. Suddenly, the fight over property lines and town ordinances seemed impossibly small. What right did I have to wield such ruinous, insignificant power?
