The silence was the worst part. Before the carefully constructed words, before the practiced calm he would have to wear like armor, there was only this hollow echo in the vast conference room. He adjusted the microphone, a pointless, trembling gesture. It was a role. A necessary, solitary performance where he had to embody a certainty he had not felt in weeks, replaying the decision over and over in the dead hours of the night. Every eye in that room would soon be a small, sharp weight, and he could already feel the collective gravity of their careers, their mortgages, their quiet hopes, all pulling him down. His job now was not to lead them to a brighter future, but to be the steady, unbreaking surface upon which their present would shatter. He had to absorb the impact first.
