He slid the document across the rough wooden table, the crisp paper a stark white against the dark grain. “It’s the official survey, signed and stamped. The lines are clear.” She didn’t even glance at the map, her eyes fixed on his face, searching for something she no longer recognized. “My grandmother’s bones lie under what your ink calls Plot C,” she stated, her voice devoid of emotion. He sighed, a sound of deep frustration. “This is for our own good! It gives us a title, a claim they have to recognize in their courts.” He leaned forward, pleading. “We have to use their tools to survive.” “You speak their words of ownership now,” she said, her voice dropping to a low, steady whisper. “You think a piece of paper can hold this land? This land holds us. It remembers every season, every birth, every burial. What will your paper remember when the ink fades and the men who signed it are dust?”
