“Hey,” Maria said softly, resting a hand on the back of his empty chair. David didn’t look up from the darkened monitor, where his own tired reflection stared back. “Don’t listen to what they’re saying in there. It was a long shot from the start.” He finally turned, and his usual easy confidence was gone, replaced by a brittle quietness. “But I sold them on it, Maria. I stood in front of the board and told them it was the future. I convinced them.” He let out a dry, humourless laugh. “For months, that project was my entire identity. Now when I walk down the hall, I can feel it. I’m not the guy with the big idea anymore.” He looked down at his hands, spread flat on the desk. “I’m just the guy who was wrong about everything.”
