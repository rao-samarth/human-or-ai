The phone hit the pavement with a sickening crack. Silence. Then, a roar. He didn’t bend to retrieve the shattered pieces. He just spun, his face a mask of outrage, and jabbed a finger into the moving crowd. “He pushed me! He did it on purpose!” His mother, a step behind, flinched not at the accident but at his fury. The accusation was wild, baseless, but she didn’t question it. “Sweetheart, it’s okay, we’ll go right now,” she pleaded, already fumbling for her purse, her voice a desperate whisper against the city’s blare. Pedestrians swerved around them, a river parting for a jagged, unmovable stone. But he wasn’t listening. His glare swept the anonymous faces, searching for a target, for someone to hold accountable for a problem he had created and refused to own.
