He stood across the street, watching the people flow in and out of the granite entrance like a tide. For two decades, that building had been a monument to his powerlessness, a cold stone fortress guarding a buried truth. Today, it was just a destination. The city's cacophony—the screech of tires, the layered chatter of a thousand conversations—felt impossibly distant, a memory from someone else's life. His own world had shrunk to this single block, this final crossing. He felt the worn envelope in his coat pocket, its contents the only compass he had possessed through all the silent years. The light changed. He took a breath, the city air sharp and metallic, and stepped off the curb, moving not with the crowd but through it, a solitary figure on a collision course with a history that had waited long enough.
