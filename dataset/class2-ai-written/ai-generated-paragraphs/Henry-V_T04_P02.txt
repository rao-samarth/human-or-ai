From his usual bench by the town’s war memorial, he watched the annual summer festival unfold on the green. He remembered when the food stalls sold only hot dogs and lemonade, their signs painted by the same handful of families. Now, the air was thick with the unfamiliar, delicious scents of spiced meats on a vertical spit and sweet fried dough he couldn’t name, mingling with the familiar smell of grilled corn. A group of children, some with his grandfather’s pale hair and others with hair as dark as night, chased each other with small, brightly colored flags, their laughter a chaotic mix of languages. He saw the new mayor, whose parents came from a country he once only knew from maps, shaking hands with a farmer whose family had worked this valley for two centuries. It wasn't about erasing the old story, he realized, but about the story getting bigger, folding new, complicated pages into itself right here on the grass.
