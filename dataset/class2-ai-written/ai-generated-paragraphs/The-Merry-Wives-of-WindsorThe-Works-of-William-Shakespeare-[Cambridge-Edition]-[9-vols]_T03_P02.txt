He cornered her by the cafe's steamed-up window, his expensive coat a dark shadow against the morning light. “You’re making this difficult,” he said, his voice a low murmur meant only for her. “Unnecessarily so. It’s a simple zoning variance.” She clutched her worn leather bag, its strap digging into her shoulder like an anchor. “Simple for you. You sign a check from an office a hundred miles away, and a century of this town’s history disappears. We’re the ones who have to live with the empty space.” He sighed, a theatrical display of exhausted patience. “My family has guided this community for generations. We understand what progress requires.” She finally met his gaze, her own clear and steady. “You don’t guide anything. You own things. And you think that gives you the right to break the rules the rest of us live by. Well, some things aren’t for sale. My integrity, for one. This town, for another.”
