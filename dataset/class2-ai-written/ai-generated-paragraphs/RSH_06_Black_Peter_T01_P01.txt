The two men at the counter spoke in low tones, but the words carried over the hiss of the griddle. “So, they finally found old Silas,” one said, not looking up from his newspaper. His companion grunted, wiping his mouth with a napkin. “In the alley behind the cannery. Not a pretty sight, from what I hear.” They both knew the man, or at least his reputation. He was a creature of hard angles and harder bargains, a man who’d once shattered a friend’s kneecap for a twenty-dollar debt. He’d navigated the city’s underbelly for decades with a snarling kind of tenacity, believing strength was the only currency that mattered. There was a certain grim justice, then, that his end came not in a blaze of glory but in a puddle of grimy rainwater with a cheap knife sticking out from his worn-out coat. All that ferocity and fear he’d cultivated over the years bled out into the gutter, leaving nothing behind but a story for strangers to trade over breakfast.
