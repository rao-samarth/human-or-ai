He’d let the silence stretch for so long they must have mistaken it for agreement. He finally looked up, his gaze sweeping over their expectant faces before settling on the dust motes catching the last of the sun. His voice, when it came, wasn't loud, but it scraped the air in the quiet barn, raw with a feeling he’d never shown them before. “You see the deed, the line on the map. You think this is about ownership.” He shook his head, a small, tired gesture. “I watch the deer starve when the creek runs low. I see the soil turn to powder. You think I broke something? I was trying to fix what was already broken.” This wasn't about a single season's crop or a disputed boundary; it was about the simple, honest truth of the place. It was his entire life, his whole quiet existence, suddenly poured into the space between them, and for the first time, it felt like they were seeing the man who had stood there all along.
