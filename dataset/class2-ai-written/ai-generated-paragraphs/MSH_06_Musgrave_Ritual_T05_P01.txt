From his new, smaller kitchen window, he watched them drive the first stakes into the north pasture. The quiet he had always taken for granted was now punctuated by the beep of a reversing truck. He had expected tears, perhaps a few bitter letters he could burn. He had not expected this calculated dismantling of his entire world. She hadn't fought him for the house; that would have been a battle of passion. Instead, she had quietly acquired the adjacent lands, leveraged his debts, and sold the entire parcel—his ancestral home included—to a consortium that planned to build identical, beige-sided family homes. She wasn't burning it down. She was erasing it, turning centuries of his family's history into a suburban cul-de-sac named something blandly aspirational, like 'Willow Creek Estates'. It was a far more profound violence, a patient and absolute annihilation of memory itself.
