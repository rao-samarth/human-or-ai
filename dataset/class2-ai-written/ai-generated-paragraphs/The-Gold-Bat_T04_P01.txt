The ceiling fan spun in slow, silent circles above his bed. He replayed his father's question for the tenth time, the words calm but heavy, delivered at the bottom of the stairs an hour ago. He could still see the spray of blue ceramic across the floor, could still see Leo's face, pale and frozen in the sudden silence that followed the crash. The answer was simple, but saying it out loud was impossible. It was a line you just didn't cross, a law more real than any rule written in a school handbook. You don't give up a friend, especially not when he was already on thin ice at home. But the look on his own dad's face—that quiet expectation of honesty—was its own kind of pressure, a different and more complicated weight. A third path began to form in his mind, a simple, terrifying sentence he could almost taste: It was me. The lie felt cleaner, somehow more honorable, than the truth.
