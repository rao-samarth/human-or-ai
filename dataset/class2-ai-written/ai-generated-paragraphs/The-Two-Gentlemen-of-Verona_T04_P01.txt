She knelt by the overgrown rose bush, the damp soil seeping into the knees of her coarse trousers. The wide-brimmed straw hat, a cheap market find, cast a convenient shadow over her features, but the real work was in the details. It was in the deliberate way she slouched, a subtle rounding of shoulders that contradicted a lifetime of poised discipline, and in the slight, practiced roughness of her accent when she’d thanked the delivery boy for the morning paper. Her hands, gloveless, were stained with earth and pricked by thorns—a small, necessary price for authenticity. A neighbor strolled past the low stone wall, offering a simple nod which she returned with a hesitant, tired smile that wasn't entirely her own. The person who lived here was quiet, unassuming, a woman who simply tended her garden. And in this meticulously crafted anonymity, she had found the most effective hiding place of all: plain sight.
