She cornered him by the half-empty punch bowl, the sound of their cousins laughing in the other room a bitter counterpoint. "You look so pleased with yourself," she said, her voice low and tight. He straightened his expensive tie, a small, smug smile playing on his lips. "It was a good quarter. The board is happy." "And Frank?" she pressed, the name a sharp stone thrown into his placid satisfaction. "Is Frank happy, living out of his car because you gutted his division?" His smile finally faltered. "That's business. Collateral damage. You wouldn't understand." She shook her head, a single, sharp motion of disgust. "I understand that you used to be someone who cared about people. Now you just see them as rungs on a ladder. What's next? Who else do you have to kick down to get to the very top? I hope it's lonely up there. I truly do."
