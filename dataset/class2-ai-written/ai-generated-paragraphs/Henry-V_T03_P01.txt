He didn't raise his voice. That was the most unnerving part. Instead, he leaned back in his leather chair, the old material groaning softly under his weight, and steepled his fingers. For a long moment, he simply watched me, his gaze unhurried, as if I were a curious specimen under glass. He then picked up a fountain pen from the polished expanse of his desk, turning it over and over, its gold nib catching the lamplight. The gesture was slow, measured, and utterly dismissive. It wasn't an act of distraction; it was a demonstration. He was showing me, with every calibrated movement, that my urgency was irrelevant, that the entire conversation would proceed at a pace of his choosing. When he finally spoke, his voice was quiet, almost gentle, which only amplified the cold finality of his decision.
