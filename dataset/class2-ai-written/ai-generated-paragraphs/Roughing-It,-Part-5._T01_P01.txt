Work had effectively stopped, replaced by a low, electric hum of shared delusion. No one spoke of deadlines or clients anymore; conversations were hushed, frantic exchanges of ticker symbols and acronyms that meant nothing to me but everything to them. Mark, from accounting, hadn't looked away from his phone in three days. His face, illuminated by the ceaseless climb of a single green line, was a mask of ecstatic terror. He’d grab your arm, his eyes wide and pupils pinned, muttering about monumental gains and how this was their one chance, their only chance. Spreadsheets lay forgotten, project plans gathered digital dust. We were all standing on a cliff's edge, but they were the only ones cheering, convinced that if they just believed hard enough, they would suddenly learn to fly. The air was thick with it—the intoxicating, terrifying poison of hope untethered from reality.
