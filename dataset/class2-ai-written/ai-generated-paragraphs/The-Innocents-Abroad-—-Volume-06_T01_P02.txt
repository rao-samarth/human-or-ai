He watched her expression as he spoke, a familiar and subtle deflation. She had imagined the Whispering Canyons as they were in the old travelogues: a sacred gorge where the wind sang melodies through rose-colored stone. He, however, remembered the eight-hour bus ride on a cracked vinyl seat, the overpriced water bottles sold at the entrance, and the insistent buzz of flies. The actual "whispering" had been a thin, unremarkable whistle, drowned out by the chatter of other visitors all vying for the one perfect angle for a photo. The legend, he understood, was always more resilient than the landmark, a perfect, unchanging idea immune to bad weather and commercialization. He saw in her eyes the pristine, imagined landscape she so wanted to believe in, a place far more compelling than the dusty, sun-bleached reality he had actually walked through. He trailed off, leaving her with the shimmer of a dream meeting its dull, factual end.
