My grandfather’s voice filled the dining room, recounting the old story for what must have been the hundredth time. We all played our parts, a captive audience to the man at the head of the table whose word had always been law. But then he faltered, placing the decisive event in August when we all knew it was September. A flicker of something—was it panic?—crossed his eyes before he forged ahead with his version. My aunt shot me a warning glance from across the polished wood, her fork frozen mid-air. The air grew thick with what wasn't being said. To correct him felt like more than just an interruption; it felt like a coup, a direct challenge to the very structure of our family. The foundation of these gatherings was his certainty, his infallible memory. We all knew his authority was absolute, but in that moment, we also felt how thin the ice was, how easily a single, spoken truth could send us all plunging into the cold.
