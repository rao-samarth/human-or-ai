He’d been standing in the queue for the observation deck for what felt like three years, the sun relentlessly baking the back of his neck. He was doing it for them, of course. For the memories. His seven-year-old daughter, who had been mercifully sitting in the shade, skipped over and presented her masterpiece, a crayon drawing on a crumpled napkin. There was Mommy, a radiant sun with flowing yellow hair. There was her brother, a caped crusader mid-flight. And there he was. A large, brown rectangle with a frowny face and two wobbly lines for legs. “That’s you, Daddy,” she explained with devastating sincerity. “You’re the suitcase. You carry all our stuff.” He looked down at the two backpacks slung over his shoulders and the water bottles clipped to his belt. The love was still there, but it was currently buried under a profound sense of being a luggage trolley with feelings.
