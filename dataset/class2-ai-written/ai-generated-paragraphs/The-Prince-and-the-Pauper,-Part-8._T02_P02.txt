For years, he had been the office's self-appointed guardian of kitchen sink etiquette, a staunch defender of the principle that a used mug's journey to the dishwasher should be immediate. It wasn't just about cleanliness; it was a matter of basic decency, a bright line between civilization and chaos. He'd mentally drafted entire manifestos on the subject during his morning commute. Then came the day of the Great Server Fire, a purely metaphorical but career-threatening inferno that had him sprinting from his half-made tea. Hours later, adrenaline spent and victory declared, he wandered back to the kitchen for water. There it sat: his own mug, a forlorn island in the stainless-steel basin, a damp tea bag string hanging over the rim like a tiny, pathetic flag of surrender. He finally understood that sometimes, the road to ruin isn't paved with laziness, but with a panicked call from the IT department.
