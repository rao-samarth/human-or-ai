You have to understand it was never just about the scholarship. It was the air we breathed from the first day, racing each other down the concrete steps to the subway platform, the screech of the train a starting pistol for another day’s contest. He was always a half-step ahead. Not enough to be a prodigy, just enough to make my own efforts feel clumsy and second-rate, a pale reflection in the glass of the towering office blocks we passed. The echo of his name announced for another student award was just as loud as the sirens that constantly wailed past our classroom window. In a city of millions, our entire world had somehow shrunk to a two-person contest where his victory was, by definition, my defeat. It became a kind of frantic, desperate physics of its own. So, yes. I switched the labels on his project. It wasn’t about the grade; it was about disrupting the orbit, just once.
