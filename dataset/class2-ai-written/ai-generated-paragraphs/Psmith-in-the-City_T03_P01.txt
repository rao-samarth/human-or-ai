Uncle Robert was holding court again, his voice booming across the polished dining table about the virtues of “common sense” over formal education. My mother began studying her fork with intense focus; my father suddenly found a wine stain on the tablecloth fascinating. “You just know things, or you don’t!” Robert declared, punctuating the thought with a soft thump of his fist. A dense silence followed, thick with unspoken disagreement. Then my cousin, who had been quietly arranging her green beans into a perfect line, looked up. She smiled faintly. “So, is it a kind of inherited knowledge, then? A wisdom that conveniently requires no reading?” Her question was delivered with such genuine, light curiosity that for a beat it seemed entirely innocent. The air shifted. Uncle Robert’s face clouded with confusion. The barb had been delivered wrapped in silk, a sharp point disguised as a soft inquiry. He sputtered, searching for a response that wasn’t an admission of his own premise, and in his searching silence, the entire argument collapsed.
