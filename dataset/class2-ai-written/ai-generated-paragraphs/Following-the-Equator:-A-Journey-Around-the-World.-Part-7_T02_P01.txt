The phone was dead. He just needed a price and a time. That was it. Instead, he got a small glass of scalding tea and a long story about a nephew’s graduation. He tapped his foot, checked his watch—a useless gesture. The old man smiled, his questions not about the phone’s warranty but about his own parents, his journey here. Every attempt to steer the conversation back to watts and repair schedules was met with a gentle, maddening detour into neighborhood gossip or the quality of this year’s olives. He was trying to complete a task, a simple item on a checklist, while the man across the counter was apparently trying to forge a multi-generational alliance. The frantic, linear logic of his own world simply dissolved here, leaving him stranded in a conversation with no clear end point.
