The low hum of the climate control was the only sound in the cavernous living room, a sterile whisper against the thick pane of the floor-to-ceiling window. Below, the city was a breathtaking deception of light. From this height, you could almost believe it was all one brilliant, thriving entity. But he knew better. He could trace the invisible borders in the darkness. There were the warm, golden clusters of their neighborhood, where every lamp post stood like a proud soldier guarding islands of manicured peace. And then, further out, the light grew thin and desperate, a fractured, grainy haze that barely held back the night. It was out there, in that vast, blurry expanse, that a different kind of life was being lived. A life he could only ever observe from behind this silent, invisible wall, separated by a gulf far deeper and wider than the miles of concrete and steel between them. A sudden, cold ache settled in his chest; it felt like watching a distant, struggling planet through a telescope, a world whose air he would never truly breathe.
