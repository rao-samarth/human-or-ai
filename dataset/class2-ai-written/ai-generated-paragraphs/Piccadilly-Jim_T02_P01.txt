“So you're actually angry because you didn't get a chance to impress a man who sells server space?” I finally said, turning from the rain-streaked window of the train. The landscape was a blur of green and grey. He didn't even look up from his phone, his thumb swiping furiously through a list of contacts. “It’s not about the server space, it’s about the access. The people he knows. You don’t get it.” His voice was tight, dismissive. “Oh, I get it,” I shot back, my own voice rising. “I get that you're sitting here cataloging human beings like assets while an entire country scrolls past the glass. You're not building a life; you're just trying to win a game no one else is even playing. A game where the prize is just a slightly better seat at a table full of people exactly like you.” He finally looked up, his expression a cold, flat wall, and for a moment, the only sound was the rhythmic clatter of the wheels on the track.
