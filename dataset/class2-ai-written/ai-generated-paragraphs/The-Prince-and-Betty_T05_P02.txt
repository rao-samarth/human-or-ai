The siren's wail ripped through the quiet afternoon, a sound almost forgotten in a town where the biggest news was a bake sale. Then came the water. It wasn't a gentle creep; it was a muddy, churning wave surging down Main Street, swallowing cars and slamming against shop windows. For months, I’d been drifting, just another ghost haunting familiar sidewalks. But this—this was different. I wasn't thinking, just moving. Grabbing sandbags from the hardware store's flooded loading dock, I was heaving them with strangers, our shouts swallowed by the roar. We formed a human chain, passing supplies toward the community hall. My muscles burned. My jeans were soaked with foul-smelling water. But for the first time in what felt like a lifetime, the fog in my head was gone, replaced by the sharp, undeniable clarity of having something vital to do. The world had narrowed to this single, frantic task, and in the chaos, I finally felt grounded.
