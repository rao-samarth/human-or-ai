He gently nudged the tiny brass gear into place, the click of its teeth engaging almost inaudible in the quiet house. A decade ago, nights like this were spent staring at spreadsheets, his mind a numb buzz of figures he couldn't make sense of. He remembered the final performance review, the manager’s strained politeness explaining that he simply didn't have the "killer instinct" for sales. The dismissal had felt like a brand on his skin for years. Now, though, with the delicate inner workings of the antique watch spread before him under the lamp's warm glow, he saw it differently. That failure hadn’t been an endpoint. It was a course correction, a violent shove away from a life he was built to admire but not to live. He had to be spectacularly bad at that to finally have permission to be good at this.
