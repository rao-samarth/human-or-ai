The earth smelled of raw mud and progress. He stood on the new embankment, listening to the satisfying gurgle of the stream being forced into his irrigation ditch. For the first time in months, he felt the knot of fear in his stomach loosen; his fields would drink deep, and the harvest would be saved. But as the sun dipped lower, casting long shadows from the silent woods, another sound began to creep in: the imagined silence from his neighbor’s land. He pictured Miller’s children watching the familiar creek shrink to a trickle, their own crops turning a brittle yellow. A single act to secure his own future had just sowed a season of ruin for others. The water was his now, a stolen lifeblood, and the quiet of the evening suddenly felt less like peace and more like an accusation.
