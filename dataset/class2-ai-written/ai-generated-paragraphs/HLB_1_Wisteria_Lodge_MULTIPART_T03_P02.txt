The steam from his paper cup fogged the lower half of the grimy station window, blurring the faces of the travelers rushing past on the platform. After seven years of transit, of deliberately misplacing his own history in cities where no one knew his name, he’d finally started to feel the anonymity he craved. It was then he saw him. Across the bustling concourse, a man with a familiar, bland face was buying a newspaper. Not a general, not a secret police chief, but a low-level bureaucrat from the records division, a man whose signature had appeared on his father’s final transfer order. As if sensing his gaze, the man looked up, his eyes scanning the crowd before they settled, for just a second, on him. There was no threat, only a flicker of dull recognition, a silent acknowledgment that distance was merely a postponement. The years evaporated. The coffee in his hand was suddenly cold, and he knew, with a certainty that hollowed out his stomach, that his new life had just become an old debt.
