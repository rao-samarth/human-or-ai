The air in the drawing-room, hitherto thick with the smoke of cigars and idle chatter, grew suddenly taut as a violin string. My uncle, his face a mask of purple indignation, levelled a trembling finger at Sinclair, who stood quite still by the mantelpiece. “You, sir, are a common thief!” the old man bellowed, his words striking the assembled company into a shocked silence. Every eye—my aunt’s, my cousins’, even the butler’s, who lingered by the door—swivelled towards me, the known companion of the accused. They expected, I have no doubt, a swift denunciation, a severing of ties for the sake of family honour. Yet, how could they comprehend the bond forged in the crucible of a dozen shared dangers? I took a single step forward, placing myself between my friend and the sea of hostile faces, my own course of action as clear and as cold as a winter’s dawn.
