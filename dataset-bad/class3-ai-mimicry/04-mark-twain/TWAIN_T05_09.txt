The lamplight made her face a soft island in the deep shadows of the kitchen, and her voice was no louder than a moth’s wing against the glass. "So you'll just go on with it, then? A lifetime spent paying a debt you don't even rightly owe?" I stopped my pacing and looked out at the impenetrable dark where the town slept, entirely ignorant of the particular brand of damnation being brewed up right here over a cold stove. "A promise is a thing that gets a hold on a man, Clara—it's got teeth and claws you don't see when you first shake hands on the bargain. I gave my word. To her, to her father, to the whole blasted congregation. To just… unravel it now? It'd be like pulling the main thread out of a sweater; the whole thing comes apart, and a man is left standing there, shivering and foolish in a pile of yarn he can't ever knit back together." She said nothing, just watched me, and that silence was a heavier judgment than any sermon I'd ever slept through.
