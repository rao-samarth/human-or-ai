I had, for the better part of an hour, been holding court from my seat on the westbound stage, enlightening my fellow passengers—a drowsy collection of farmers and dusty merchants—on matters of geology, politics, and the proper way to season a pot of beans. A man who travels much, you see, collects opinions like he collects burs on his trousers, and feels a certain duty to distribute them freely. I had just constructed a rather magnificent edifice of sagacity upon that rattling axle, a monument to my own worldly consequence, when the whole contraption gave a heave fit to un-baptize a saint. Providence, it seems, has a peculiar fondness for puncturing a man who has puffed himself up past a respectable size. My calamitous descent ended not on the floor, but squarely in a crate of live chickens bound for some unfortunate dinner table, a flurry of feathers and indignant squawking that served as the perfect punctuation to my lecture.
