Grandfather presided over the Sunday dinner table as if it were his own private kingdom, and we his loyal, if somewhat fidgety, subjects. He had a way of clearing his throat that served as a kind of royal decree, silencing all trifling chatter before he delivered his latest edict upon the family’s future. This time, the pronouncement concerned my cousin Mary’s engagement, which he declared null and void on account of some imagined slight to the family honor—a phantom injury only he could perceive. A heavy, suffocating silence fell, the kind we had all been trained since birth to endure. But then a new sound broke it. It was only Mary, placing her fork down upon her plate with a quiet, deliberate *click*, yet it rang through that dining room like a rifle shot. She did not raise her voice; she had no need. She simply looked that old tyrant in the eye and said, “No.” A single word, and the whole rickety firmament of his authority, built on decades of bluster and fear, began to tremble and crack right before our very eyes.
