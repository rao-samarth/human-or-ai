I found myself at one of those dreadfully respectable suppers where the silence between courses is louder than the clatter of the silverware. Colonel Hemlock, a man whose gut was matched only by his opinion of himself, had just made a remark concerning young Mrs. Gable's finances that was so thoroughly unkind it could have soured the cream in our coffee. A hush fell over the table, a ponderous, suffocating thing, and every soul there suddenly found the pattern on their china to be the most fascinating subject in the known world. My own blood turned to ice water. Every instinct for self-preservation—and I have a healthy stock of them, I assure you—screamed at me to study my own plate and let the moment pass. And yet, to see the color drain from that poor woman's face, well, it put a different kind of fire in my belly. It’s a curious thing, that moment when the terror of doing nothing finally outweighs the terror of doing something. I felt my fork tremble, and I knew, with a certainty that was altogether inconvenient, that I was about to open my mouth.
