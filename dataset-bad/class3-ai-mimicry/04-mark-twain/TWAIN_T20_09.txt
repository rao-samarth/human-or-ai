Aunt Clara sat at the head of the table, her face a carefully constructed mask of sorrow for the benefit of us all. The tears, when they came, were punctual as a riverboat, arriving just as the conversation veered toward some forbidden joy. She was mourning young Henry, you see—the boy who had finally fled her smothering righteousness for the honest freedom of the road. She would clasp her hands, a martyr in calico, and speak of her nightly prayers for the prodigal, her voice trembling with a practiced affliction that could make a stone weep. But I had been there the week before he vanished; I heard her tell that boy, her own flesh and blood, that his soul was a black and shriveled thing, destined for eternal fire, all because he’d had the nerve to laugh on a Sunday. Now, she played the bereaved mother for the cousins, a saintly figure enduring a trial, when the truth was she had been the trial, the very furnace from which the boy had rightly escaped.
