It was the considered opinion of the residents of Little Bimplington that Percy Frobisher had, to use the local parlance, completely lost his marbles over the affair of the giant marrow. A gentle soul whose chief ambition hitherto had been to master the more complex knots in macramé, Percy now exhibited the wild-eyed glare of a cornered badger. What began as a mild competitive spirit for the annual village fete had curdled into a round-the-clock mania; he would spend his nights crouched behind the garden shed, swaddled in a travelling rug and armed with a croquet mallet, convinced that rival horticulturalists were plotting some dreadful skulduggery. The postman reported finding him measuring the vegetable’s girth with a tailor’s tape at dawn, while muttering dark threats against the slug population—a state of affairs which caused the poor man to begin leaving Percy’s mail in a nearby hedge.
