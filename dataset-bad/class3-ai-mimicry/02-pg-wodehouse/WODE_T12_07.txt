It was under the silvery wash of a distinctly disapproving-looking moon that I found myself creeping, cat-like—or as cat-like as a fellow in squeaky brogues can manage—towards the allotment of old Colonel Pemberthy. There it squatted, the Colonel’s prize-winning marrow, a vast, green brute of a thing that seemed to be positively smirking with self-satisfaction. A chap’s conscience, of course, is meant to pipe up at moments like these, delivering a stern lecture on sportsmanship and playing with a straight bat, and mine was certainly giving it the old college try. But its well-meaning chirps were being rather drowned out by a louder, more persuasive voice
