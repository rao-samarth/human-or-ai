It was with the expansive air of a man laying down the law on a subject of which he is the sole master that Barnaby Fitzwilliam gestured a well-manicured hand toward the colossal skeleton. “The key to the creature, my dear Gwendolyn,” he boomed, ensuring the two old ladies and a family of tourists were well within earshot, “is the noble, almost Roman, arch of its magnificent proboscis.” He was about to elaborate on this zoological scoop when a thin, reedy pipe of a voice cut through the reverent hush. “That’s not its proboscis, sir, that’s its nasal cavity. And it’s an Allosaurus, not a Tyrannosaurus.” The pronouncement emanated from a small boy who looked as if he had been hatched from a dictionary. The effect on Barnaby was instantaneous and profound. He deflated, not with a bang, but with a long, wheezing sigh, like a punctured set of bagpipes.
