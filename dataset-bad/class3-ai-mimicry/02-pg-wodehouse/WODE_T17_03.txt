“I say, Bertram, old horse,” bellowed Major Grimsby from across the rose bushes, his voice carrying on the gentle breeze like a summons to the parade ground, “that new water feature of yours. It makes a most peculiar gurgling sound, what? Rather puts one in mind of my late uncle emptying his hot water bottle.” A profound and somewhat chilly silence descended upon the garden, broken only by the buzzing of a particularly stout bee. Bertram, whose Florentine-style fountain was his pride and joy—the centrepiece, as it were, of his rural paradise—lowered his pruning shears with dangerous slowness. “Are you implying, Major,” he enquired, his tone having dropped several degrees below freezing, “that the melodious cascade I personally installed is akin to the nocturnal ablutions of a gout-ridden relative?” The Major, a man whose foot was more often in his mouth than in his boot, blundered on. “No, no, dear boy! Meant it as a compliment! The… ah… the sheer volume of it!” But the damage was done. The invitation to Grimsby for that evening’s bridge was, one felt, now irretrievably off the table.
