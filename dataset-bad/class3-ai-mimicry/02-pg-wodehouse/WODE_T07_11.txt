My Uncle Theodore, having commandeered the motorcar's front passenger seat with the air of a stoutish Napoleon claiming a continent, had laid down the law regarding the dashboard wireless. There was to be, he announced, a profound and scholarly silence for the duration of our journey to Bigglesby-on-Sea; a silence conducive to the digestion of his formidable lunch and the contemplation of higher things. It was a decree, not a suggestion, and for a solid ten miles the only sound was the purr of the engine and the faint, dyspeptic rumblings from Theodore himself. Then, my cousin Agatha, a girl hitherto noted more for her skill with a water-colour brush than for raw courage, leaned forward with a look of beatific calm. With a single, decisive flick of her wrist, she twisted the knob, unleashing a torrent of Charleston that hit the car's interior with the force of a seltzer bottle to the back
