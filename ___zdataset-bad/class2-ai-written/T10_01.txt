The train rattled through the grey countryside, each click of the tracks a small, nagging metronome counting out the miles. He had told me the story of his past so simply, a clean narrative born of mutual agreement and quiet departures. He’d laid out the facts like clean, folded laundry, each one neat and in its place. But sitting here, watching the unfamiliar landscape slip by, I remembered the things he hadn't said—the brief, pained flicker in his friend's eyes when the topic came up, an old photograph I’d found with a different face smiling back. He hadn't lied, not exactly. He had just presented a version of events so carefully curated, with all the inconvenient emotions and messy details snipped away, that what remained was a hollow shape. It was a story designed not to inform, but to manage my perception of him, a quiet architecture of omission I was only now beginning to see.
