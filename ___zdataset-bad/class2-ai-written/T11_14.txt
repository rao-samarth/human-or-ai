He stood at the podium in the town hall, his prepared notes trembling in his hand. He’d practiced for a week, the words meant to save the last green space near the old cannery, the only park on his side of the river. But Mayor Abernathy didn’t look up from his phone, and Mrs. Gable, whose family had owned the bank for a century, just smiled that tight, polite smile she gave everyone whose last name wasn't on a street sign. His voice, when it finally came, felt small in the echoing hall. He spoke of his childhood, of fishing in that creek, of the community garden they’d planned. No one reacted. They weren't listening to his reasons; they were just waiting for him to finish. He wasn't a neighbor with a valid concern; he was just noise, a temporary, unseemly inconvenience before they voted to approve the luxury condos. The microphone hummed, amplifying his sudden silence, and he felt the crushing weight of generations pressing down, whispering that some people are born to be heard, while others are just meant to be quiet.
