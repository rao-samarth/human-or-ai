The clock on the mantel ticks away the minutes, but it does nothing to cool the persistent heat in a man’s own face—a private sort of conflagration that burns long after the public spectacle is over. A man can, in the space of a single evening, construct a truly magnificent monument to his own intellect, furnishing it with all manner of grand pronouncements and self-assured philosophies, believing it to be granite from foundation to spire. And yet it takes but one stray remark, one well-aimed question from a quiet corner, to expose the whole contraption for what it is: a flimsy affair of pasteboard and wind. The guests are long gone, the lamps are low, but the laughter, confound it, seems to have taken up permanent residence in the rafters. You are left alone with the wreckage, the undisputed sovereign of a kingdom
