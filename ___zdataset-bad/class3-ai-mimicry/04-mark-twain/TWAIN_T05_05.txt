He pulled the wire taut against the cedar post, the strain of it a familiar and honest ache in his shoulders. A man ought to find a kind of peace in such a task—a straightforward business of mending what’s broken. But his mind kept wandering off the property line, clear to the county seat and beyond it, where a certain laugh was easier to recall than the Lord’s own prayer on a Sunday morning. There was a popular notion, mostly found in cheap novels, that a feller could just up and follow a feeling like that, shedding his promises same as a snake sheds its skin in the spring. He reckoned, though, that the scribblers who dreamed up such tales had never stood on a piece of land that was their father’s and his father’s before, a plot of earth that depended on them for its very life. So he gave the wire another hard yank, twisting it tight. A fence, you see, will generally stay where you put it; it ain’t got a contrary opinion of its own, which is a considerable advantage it has over a man’s own confounded heart.
