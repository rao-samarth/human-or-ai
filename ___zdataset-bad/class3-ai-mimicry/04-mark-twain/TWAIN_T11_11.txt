A body could plant himself on any corner of this clamorous metropolis and, inside of an hour, learn all he needs to know about the human animal without cracking a single book. It’s a curious sort of geometry they practice, for the space a man is allowed to occupy has precious little to do with his size and everything to do with the cut of his coat or the polish on his boots. You see a fellow in fine wool part the shuffling masses just by the set of his chin—he expects the path to clear, and by some unspoken agreement, it does—while another soul, just as solid in flesh and bone, is treated like so much drifting fog, to be shouldered through without a word of apology. They all carry an invisible ledger, it seems, with their worth tallied up by an unseen clerk before they ever draw their first breath. And the most perplexing thing of all is that a man born to a page near the back of the book will spend his whole life trying to convince himself, and anyone who'll listen, that it’s the only page worth reading
