“Hark!” I said, pausing with one foot on the stile and adopting the keen, intelligent expression of a bloodhound that has just caught a promising scent. “The unmistakable chirp of the Lesser Crested Pipit, old boy. A devilish rare bird in these climes. One has to have an ear for it, you know—a certain je ne sais quoi that separates the true naturalist from the common-or-garden gawper.” A warm glow of self-satisfaction suffused the Binkerton frame; it’s not often one gets to demonstrate such specialised knowledge. Reggie, who had been wrestling with a stubborn thistle, merely grunted. “Sounds more like a squeaky gate to me,” he offered, without so much as looking up. I was on the point of delivering a short but pithy lecture on the finer points of avian acoustics when the vicar’s youngest son, a small fiend with jam on his collar, popped his head over the hedge, put two fingers in his mouth, and emitted the exact same noise. The sun seemed to go in, and the landscape took on the bleak, uninviting aspect of a Monday morning.
