It was Aunt Mildred who cornered me by the potted ferns, her expression that of a Roman emperor who has just spotted a flaw in the gladiatorial arrangements, to deliver a rather searching address on the evils of idle chatter. The trading of baseless rumour, she informed me, was a moral blight, a veritable poison that sours the cream of human kindness and what-not. A soul that stoops to such tittle-tattle, she continued, fixing me with a gimlet eye as if I spent my days collecting the stuff in a large sack, is a soul already halfway down the slippery slope to perdition. Having thus established the ground rules for decent society, she leaned in conspiratorially, the pearls at her throat clicking like tiny, impatient castanets. “And now, tell me, dear boy,” she breathed, lowering her voice to a conspiratorial whisper, “is it absolutely true that this new fellow of Cecily Bingham’s has one of those frightfully modern glass eyes, and does it, as Millicent swears, occasionally pop out if he sneezes too vigorously?”
