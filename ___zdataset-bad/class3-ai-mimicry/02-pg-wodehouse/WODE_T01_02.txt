It was generally agreed that Cousin Herbert had done frightfully well for himself, having cornered the market in some deucedly important commodity—gilded doorstops or whatnot—and was now, to all intents and purposes, rolling in the stuff. Yet, as he stood by the mantelpiece, a solitary pillar of fiscal rectitude amidst the general hubbub of Aunt Mildred’s annual garden party, one couldn’t help but notice a certain something amiss. He had, it seemed, climbed his particular ladder with such single-minded assiduity that he’d neglected to bring the rest of his personality along with him for the final ascent. His younger relations gave him a wide berth, as if his starched collar might bite, and even his own sister spoke to him in the carefully measured tones one reserves for a visiting dignitary or a temperamental boiler. He had most certainly secured the bag, as the modern chaps say, but appeared in the process to have entirely mislaid the instructions for what to do with it once the securing was done.
