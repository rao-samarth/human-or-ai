The air in Lady Agatha’s rose garden was thick with the scent of summer and, for young Reginald, the even thicker miasma of a soul in torment. He squared his shoulders, a maneuver which did little to project an air of stern authority and rather more to make him resemble a startled penguin. "I saw you," he began, his voice a sort of strangled croak, "hobnobbing with that chap Fitzwilliam. By the sundial. He was laughing." Penelope, who had been calmly dead-heading a prize-winning bloom, paused and turned, her expression one of mild, scientific interest. "He was, Reggie. He’d just told a rather amusing story about a parrot. It seems you feel this constitutes a hanging offence?" Reginald deflated like a punctured tyre.
