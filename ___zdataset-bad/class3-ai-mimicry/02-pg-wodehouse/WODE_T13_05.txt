It was clear that Percival had entered his uncle’s study operating under a considerable misapprehension; chiefly, the misapprehension that his scheme for breeding luminous newts was a sound financial venture. He had begun his pitch with a jaunty air, all flashing teeth and confident gestures, while the elder relative merely regarded him from behind the desk with the benevolent neutrality of a particularly stern gargoyle. But as the uncle, in a voice devoid of all emotion, began to poke methodical holes in the proposition—a jab here at logistical impossibilities, a prod there at the simple lack of a market for glowing amphibians—one could practically watch the starch go out of the man. The chest, once puffed like a pouter pigeon’s, gradually deflated; the oratorical flow dwindled to a miserable trickle, and the general aspect shifted from that of a captain of industry to a small boy who has been caught using the vicar’s top hat as a boat.
