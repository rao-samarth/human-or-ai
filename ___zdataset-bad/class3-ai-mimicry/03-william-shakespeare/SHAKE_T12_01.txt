Look you, Agnes, what the ploughshare hath turned up from the earth’s dark slumber,” said Edmund, holding forth a clasp of tarnished silver set with a single, murky stone. His wife snatched it from his calloused hand, her eyes widening as she rubbed the grime away. “A devil’s bauble, I do think, and best returned to the soil whence it came,” he muttered, but she heard him not. “Returned? Art thou mad, husband?” she whispered, her voice a sharp hiss against the evening quiet. “This is no devil’s work, but providence! See how it drinks the fading light! Such a stone might buy the north field, and the flock to graze it, freeing thy back from this endless toil for a master’s gain.” Edmund shook his head. “To keep what is not ours is to plant a seed of foulest weed in an honest garden; it will choke the soul.” She clutched the jewel to her breast, her knuckles white. “Then let my soul be choked, so long as my belly may be full.
