Sir Gideon turned the pearl earring in his thick fingers, his brow a thundercloud of practiced thrift. “What new extravagance is this?” he did lament, his voice a miser’s groan quite fit for the public stage. “This house shall fall to ruin by such vanities, good wife, for you would drain a king’s own treasury for a moment’s glitter.” He spoke of prudence as a holy writ, a sermon of lean purses and patched cloaks he preached to all the world with a most solemn face. But his lady, knowing well his ledger of secrets, held her tongue; she knew of the rare foreign wines decanted only for his private thirst, of the fat bribes that greased his ventures at the port, and of the silken favour, bought for a small fortune, that did adorn the throat of a certain songbird at the playhouse. His own virtue was a cheap cloak worn only in the wind, to be cast off in the warmth of his own hidden comforts.
