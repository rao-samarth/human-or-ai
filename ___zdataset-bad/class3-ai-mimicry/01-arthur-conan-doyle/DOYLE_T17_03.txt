It began, as such domestic catastrophes so often do, with a trifle—a single, ill-heard remark passed across the dinner table concerning young Alistair’s new enterprise. The elder Mr Croft, a man not given to subtlety, had intended a jest; yet to the sensitive ears of his nephew, the words landed with the force of a studied insult. A sharp, intemperate reply was returned, which in turn provoked a thunderous retort from the uncle, whose pride was now most grievously wounded. What had been a murmur of polite conversation but a moment before now fractured into partisan whispers and outright remonstrations. Hitherto unbreakable bonds of kinship seemed to dissolve in the heated air of that parlour, all upon the flimsy foundation of a misplaced word, a wound to vanity, and a mutual refusal to concede a single inch of ground. The schism, once formed, was never to be healed, and two branches of the family went their separate ways from that night forward.
