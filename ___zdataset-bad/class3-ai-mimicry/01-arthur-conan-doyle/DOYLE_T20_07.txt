We plunged into the torrent of humanity that choked the narrow artery of the street, our pursuit demanding every ounce of our concentration. It was then, as a carriage forced us against the grimy brickwork of a public-house, that I observed a scene of the most profound and yet commonplace absurdity. There stood Alderman Finch, that famed crusader for civic order and the proprieties of public conduct, a man whose letters to the papers decried the slightest hint of coarseness among the lower classes. He was, however, not engaged in some charitable distribution or moral lecture; rather, his face was a mask of purple fury as he bellowed at a poor wretch of a newsboy who had inadvertently jostled him, his cane raised in a manner that suggested his civic principles did not extend to his own volatile temper.
