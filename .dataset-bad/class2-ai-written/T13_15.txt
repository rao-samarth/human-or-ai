For twenty years, Dale’s garage had been the final word on any engine trouble in the county. He carried himself with the unshakeable certainty of a man who understood the secret language of pistons and spark plugs, a knowledge that set him apart. So when the gleaming, cherry-red convertible was towed in, he had chuckled, waving off the owner’s concerns with a greasy thumb and promising it would be purring by lunchtime. But the afternoon sun slanted low, and the engine remained stubbornly silent. The rhythmic clank of his wrench had slowed, becoming hesitant, then stopping altogether. It was young Kevin, the kid he barely trusted to sweep the floors, who finally pointed a shy finger at a loose wire tucked behind the distributor cap. A hot flush crept up Dale's neck, more searing than the heat from any engine block, as the crowd of onlookers went quiet. The simple, obvious fix, laid bare for all to see, had rendered his decades of expertise suddenly hollow.
