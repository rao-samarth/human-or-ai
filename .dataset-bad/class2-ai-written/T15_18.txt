“You can’t keep doing this,” David whispered, his voice tight with a strange mix of fear and pity. “This is different.” He gestured at the wall, at the constellation of photographs connected by colored yarn, the meticulous notes detailing bus schedules and lunch orders. “It’s just data,” she said, not looking away from her creation. “Patterns. If I can just understand the system, I can predict him. I can know what he needs before he does.” She traced a red line from a coffee shop receipt to a photo of him laughing with a friend. “See? The double espresso means he’s meeting Mark. It always does. He isn’t just randomly living; he’s communicating something, and I just have to learn the language.” David backed away slowly, the pins and strings on the wall starting to look less like a map and more like the intricate web of a spider, with her sitting calmly at its center.
