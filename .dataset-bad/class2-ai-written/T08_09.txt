At a small table near the rain-streaked window of the cafe, a young woman slammed her mug down, the ceramic clattering against the saucer. "He needs to pay," she insisted, her voice a low, furious tremor. "What the court decided wasn't enough. It's not fair." Across from her, an older man with tired eyes calmly stirred his tea. "What you want isn't about fairness, though," he said, his tone gentle but firm. "You want him to feel what you felt. That's a different thing entirely." The woman fell silent, staring into her coffee as if the answer floated in the dark liquid. She wanted a reckoning, a balancing of the emotional ledger that the law, with its cold, sterile logic, could never provide. But the man was right; her desire had shifted from a need for equilibrium to a raw hunger for retribution, and in the quiet space between them, the question of who truly had the authority to draw that line hung unanswered in the air.
