The argument at the circulation desk had lowered to a tense whisper, yet it seemed to fill the entire atrium. A woman, clutching a worn tote bag, pointed a trembling finger at a page in the returned history book. "It was not like this," she insisted, her voice tight with a control that was clearly failing. "I would never be so careless." The librarian, a young man with tired eyes, sighed and repeated the policy on damages for the third time. He knew he could just waive the seven-dollar fee and move on to the next person in the growing line, but she wasn't arguing about the money anymore. It was the principle, the small, sharp injustice of being marked down as negligent. To simply pay would be an admission of a fault she refused to carry, a tiny, permanent stain on her record that felt, in that moment, utterly unbearable. He could see it in the set of her jaw; she would stand there all afternoon if she had to, defending the kind of person she was.
