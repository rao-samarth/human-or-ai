The rusty pump handle was cold under his palm, a stark contrast to the quiet heat building in his chest. In his jacket pocket, the folded surveyor’s map felt impossibly heavy. It showed the truth—or at least, a version of it. A forgotten easement, a legal ghost from a century ago, gave him clear claim to the south meadow, the one with the creek that his sister, Eleanor, had always adored. She trusted him completely to divide their father’s land fairly; she wouldn’t even think to check the old county records. The thought was a splinter, working its way deeper. He could just file the paperwork. No arguments, no fuss. The best land would be his. A profound quiet had fallen over the fields, the kind of stillness that makes a man hear his own heart beating a frantic, selfish rhythm against his ribs as the rustling of dry corn stalks began to sound like a whispered accusation.
