The projector’s light cast his shadow, large and certain, against the screen behind him. He had owned this room for the last fifteen minutes, his voice a smooth and confident narration of his own genius. Every line of code, every elegant solution, was a monument to his skill, and the rapt attention of the executives was the validation he craved. "And with one final click," he announced, a theatrical flourish in his gesture, "we launch." He pressed the key. The silence that followed was not the awe he expected. It was a dead, hollow quiet as the screen, instead of bursting into life, flooded with incomprehensible red text—a brutal obituary for his project. A hot flush crept up his neck, burning his ears. His mind went blank. He could feel hundreds of eyes on him, no longer admiring, but witnessing a spectacular, public demolition of the very pedestal he had so carefully built for himself.
