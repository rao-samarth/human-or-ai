The Inspector watched him closely, this quiet man whose hands now rested so steadily upon the arms of his chair. It seemed a physiological impossibility that this same individual had, not an hour before, faced a peril which would have unmanned a regiment. Albright, for his part, offered a slight, almost weary shake of the head when the question was put to him. “The animal part of a man, Inspector, does its duty—it shrieks, it claws for retreat, it floods the veins with ice. That is a given, a constant in the equation of survival. The question, then, is whether another faculty, the one which calculates and wills, can maintain its ascendancy over the brute instinct. In that moment, I can only say that my mind was a house divided; one inhabitant was a gibbering wretch, whilst the other—the master of the house, if you will—was entirely occupied with the singular business of seeing the matter through.”
