Observe the crier on the corner, whose lungs serve as the first and most potent filter of the day’s intelligence. He does not trade in outright falsehoods, for such clumsy artifice is swiftly exposed and disdained by a public which prides itself upon its shrewdness. Rather, his particular craft is one of meticulous omission and of calculated emphasis; a house-fire which has claimed a life is not presented as a simple tragedy, but as a direct consequence of some new civic policy he wishes to see condemned. He proffers a single, stark brick and convinces the hurried passer-by that he has shown them the entire edifice. Thus, a dozen competing versions of the morning’s events are dispatched into the city’s bloodstream before the first church-bell has rung for nine, each one possessing a kernel of veracity, yet none containing the whole of the matter.
