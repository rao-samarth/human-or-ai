Amidst the glitter of the Ambassador’s ball, my attention was drawn to General Alistair Finch, whose ramrod-straight posture seemed a feat of prodigious will. I knew, as did a select few, of the calamitous financial ruin he had only just averted—a ruin brought on by his insistence on settling a debt for which no legal instrument existed, merely the breath of a whispered promise. To see him now, his countenance a mask of serene indifference whilst conversing with the Duchess, was to witness a remarkable performance. A man’s good name, I reflected, is an edifice built not of stone but of such costly, invisible sacrifices; it is his most valuable currency and his most fragile possession, demanding a vigilance so absolute that it might drain the very soul, leaving behind little more than an unimpeachable but hollow shell.
