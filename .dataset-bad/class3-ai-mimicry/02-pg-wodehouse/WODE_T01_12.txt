Surveying young Bovington from across the crowded dining hall, one couldn't help but feel a certain detached admiration for the fellow's rather meteoric ascent. There he was, chatting affably to a brace of whiskered old dugouts who controlled half the shipping in the country, looking for all the world like a cat who'd not only got the cream but had also secured the long-term contract for its supply. It was a rummy business, though, when you considered the facts; for in his spirited scramble up the greasy pole, he seemed to have mislaid quite a few of the things a chap generally holds dear—old pals, a decent sense of sportsmanship, and the ability to laugh at a risqué limerick. He had undeniably arrived, collecting all the necessary trophies and whatnot, yet there was a curious hollowness behind the eyes, as if he’d successfully won the grand prize in a raffle only to discover it was an oversized, gilt-edged photograph of himself, all alone.
