Reginald had me pinioned against a rather militant-looking aspidistra, his voice a low hiss that just about managed to cut through the din of the string quartet assassinating a popular foxtrot. “It’s childishly simple, old man,” he breathed, the scent of expensive cigars and moral elasticity wafting from his person. “A mere whisper in the right ear, a temporary reallocation of your dear aunt’s surplus assets, and we’re both sipping something tall and cool on a sun-drenched Riviera.” My brain, a notoriously feeble instrument at the best of times, began to perform a sort of dizzy cakewalk. One part of it—the bit that remembered sermons and the steely glint in my solicitor’s eye—was waving a red flag of frantic disapproval.
