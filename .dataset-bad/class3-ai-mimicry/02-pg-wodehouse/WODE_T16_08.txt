It’s a rummy sort of business, this business of buckling to,” Algernon observed, polishing his monocle with a thoughtful air. “One moment, the old spine is a perfectly serviceable column of bone and gristle, and the next, upon merely contemplating an interview with a chap like your uncle Wilberforce, it transforms into something more akin to a string of over-boiled macaroni. It isn’t, you understand, a fundamental lack of what-it-takes; rather, it’s a temporary mislaying of the vital stuff, like misplacing one's collar-stud just before a crucial dinner. The intellect perceives the precipice and quite reasonably advises the feet to remain rooted, while some deeper, more primitive part of the chump yearns for decisive forward movement. The whole affair becomes a contest of wills, you see—the cool, calculating brain versus the quivering jelly of the soul, with the grand prize being the simple ability to walk across a carpet without one’s knees imitating a pair of castanets.
