The sun had finally clocked off for the evening, leaving the garden in that murky, in-between light where every rose bush looks like a crouching beast of the field. It was the sort of quiet that sinks right into the bones, a sticky, mournful silence that made a fellow feel he ought to be composing a sonnet or at least thinking deep thoughts about the fleeting nature of things. My own thoughts, however, were resolutely refusing to be deep; they were circling, with a sort of morbid fascination, the telephone that sat on the hall table, its black form a silent reproach. To lift the receiver felt, at that moment, a task on par with wrestling a grizzly bear, and considerably less appealing. One’s nerve, I’ve found, isn’t a fixed asset; it’s more of a temperamental jelly that wobbles alarmingly when the really important conversations loom. And this one, I knew, was a beast.
