The moment, as they say in the more harrowing sort of penny dreadful, had come. Beyond that slab of mahogany, old Mr Witherspoon was waiting, a man whose smile was rumoured to curdle milk at twenty paces. My own internal arrangements, I confess, had dissolved into a sort of agitated soup; the heart hammered against the ribs like a trapped woodpecker, while the stomach performed a series of gymnastic feats that would have earned it a medal in a less civilised society. To turn and bolt for the lifts was the siren-call of the sensible soul, a perfectly rational response to impending doom. Yet, something—a lingering shred of what my old headmaster used to call 'moral fibre', though it felt more like sheer, mulish stupidity—kept my feet glued to the spot. I took a breath, a shaky, inadequate affair, and with the grim resolution of a man about to ask a wasp for a small loan, I reached for the handle.
