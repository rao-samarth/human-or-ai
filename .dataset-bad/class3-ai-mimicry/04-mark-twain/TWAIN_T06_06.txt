Young William Fletcher was suffering a peculiar sort of spiritual indigestion, the kind that comes from watching your neighbor prosper with an unholy swiftness. There was Amos Peters, not a lick more deserving than William himself, who had just acquired a new buggy with wheels painted a shade of red so brilliant it fairly insulted the Lord's own sunset. William would sit on his porch, whittling a stick down to a nub, and calculate the injustices of a world that would grant such a gaudy chariot to a man like Amos. And just like that, the sourness vanished, for it is a stubborn and pitiable resentment indeed that cannot be cured by a simple invitation and the promise of a fine day's fishing.
