There is a peculiar species of silence, a profound and heavy quiet, that only follows after a man of prodigious certainty—such as our own self-appointed Colonel Abercrombie, who was at that moment elucidating the finer points of South American geography to the captive audience of the barbershop—has made a proclamation of incontrovertible fact. He declared, with a thump of his fist upon the armrest, that the mighty Amazon River did discharge its considerable volume into the placid waters of the Pacific. It was then that a small, bespectacled woman, a schoolmarm by all appearances, looked up from her book and said, quite plainly, “I believe you will find it is the Atlantic, sir.” Now, a man in such a position has two roads and only two: he can swallow the bitter pill of his own error with a show of good grace, or he can attempt to argue with a map.
