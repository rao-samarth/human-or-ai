It was a thing of no consequence, you understand—a simple dispute over which cousin had broken the porcelain doll on a summer’s day some forty years gone. A mere puff of air, a vapor of a memory. Yet, in the stifling quiet of that parlor, this trifle took on a monstrous life of its own. First came the polite contradiction, then the firmer insistence, and soon the hot flush of indignation that announces a man believes his very honor is under assault. Old Martin, you see, did not hear his brother’s correction; he heard a judgment on the whole of his recollection, an indictment of his truthfulness from the cradle to that very dinner table. And so it went, each word a fresh shovelful of dirt upon the coffin of their fraternity, until two men who had shared a womb now stood on opposite shores of a chasm dug by a forgotten toy, their wives weeping and the roast growing cold—a whole family’s peace murdered by a ghost.
