It has long been my private conviction that the Almighty possesses a keener appreciation for a grand fiasco than for a job done properly; for there is no spectacle on this earth quite so edifying as that of a dozen sensible men diligently constructing a calamity out of the best intentions. Take the recent matter of the company’s “Centralization Initiative”—a memorandum of such profound vagueness it could mean anything or nothing at all. Mr. Abernathy, a man whose earnestness far outstripped his sense, interpreted it to mean that every ledger from every department must henceforth occupy a single, central table. What followed was a solemn procession of clerks, each hoisting a heavy volume, all converging on a small oak desk that groaned in protest. They proceeded with the gravity of surgeons, adding their burdens to the precarious mountain of bound volumes, never once questioning the enterprise, until the whole office was brought to a standstill around this new monument to misapplied diligence.
