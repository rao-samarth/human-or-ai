He stood there with his jaw clamped so tight a man might’ve struck a match on it, and I saw right then that the horse was not only out of the barn but had galloped clean into the next county. I had used the word “supposition,” you see—a simple and honest word meant only to convey that his plan rested on a trifle more hope than fact. But to his ear, inflamed as it was by a week of sleepless worry, it sounded a great deal like “deception.” And from that single, miserable seed, a whole forest of villainy had sprung up in his mind, with me as the chief scoundrel hacking at the roots of his good name. Now, what was a body to do? To explain myself further would be to confess my own clumsiness with language—a high crime in itself—and appear a backtracking coward for it. To hold my tongue, however, was to let this poison fester until it killed a brotherhood of thirty years; and for what? For a word he had caught by the wrong handle.
