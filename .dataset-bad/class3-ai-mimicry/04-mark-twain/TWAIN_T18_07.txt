The family had assembled with the sort of pious solemnity one usually reserves for a hanging—which, considering the contents of my late uncle's last testament, was not entirely inappropriate. A funereal silence had fallen, broken only by the wheezing of the lawyer, a man whose spectacles magnified his eyes to the size of dinner plates, giving him the startled look of an owl that had just witnessed a barn fire. He cleared his throat with a sound like rocks grinding in a dry creek bed and, with trembling fingers, unfolded the sacred document. He began to read, his voice quivering with manufactured emotion: “First, take six ears of fresh corn and a goodly knob of butter…” A profound stillness descended upon the room. My cousin Eliphas, who had been expecting a lumber mill, looked as if he’d been struck by a bolt of lightning that had, on its way down, also insulted his mother.
