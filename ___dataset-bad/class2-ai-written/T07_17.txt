The Minister raised his crystal glass, the light fracturing through it into a thousand tiny rainbows on the damask tablecloth. "To the centenary of our accord," he projected, his voice smooth and practiced. A wave of motion swept the hall as hundreds of guests rose, a symphony of rustling fabric and the gentle scrape of chairs. But near the back, by the tall, arched windows, one table remained still. The playwright, the poet, and the sculptor did not move. They simply sat, their hands resting in their laps, their gazes fixed not on the Minister but on some point beyond him. Their silence was not aggressive, yet it was a void in the room's adulation. The clinking of glasses from the standing majority seemed to hesitate, the cheerful sound suddenly tinny and hollow. The Minister's smile tightened at the edges, a small, almost imperceptible crack in the polished facade of the evening.
