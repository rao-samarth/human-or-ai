It was supposed to be a simple act of reverence, moving their mother’s old armoire into the sunlight for the first time in decades. The dolly wheel snapping was the initial betrayal, a sharp crack that echoed the breaking of their resolve. What followed was a strained, grunting ballet of ineptitude as the two brothers tried to wrestle the monstrous piece of furniture across the lawn. George’s foot slipped in the damp grass, and the entire weight shifted with a groan of stressed mahogany. It began its slow, majestic tilt. This was no longer just furniture; it was a monument to their failure, a dark monolith arcing with agonizing slowness toward the one thing their father cherished more than silence: his prize-winning pumpkin. They were frozen in a silent, heaving tableau, a heroic and utterly pointless struggle against gravity and their own poor planning, sweat stinging their eyes as the polished wood pointed directly at the heart of the gourd. The quiet afternoon held its breath, waiting for the inevitable, tragic crunch.
