The office was empty, but David barely noticed. His world had shrunk to the glowing rectangle of his monitor and the single highlighted cell within the Q3 financial forecast. It was a rounding discrepancy of seven cents. Immaterial, his manager had called it, with a dismissive wave. Yet, for the past week, those seven cents had become the focal point of his existence. He’d run a dozen simulations, each one confirming the same trivial error, the same minuscule fracture in the system’s integrity. He knew, on some logical level, that no one cared. He saw the way his colleagues’ eyes glazed over when he tried to explain the principle of it, the systemic rot it represented. Now he was drafting a new memo, this one for the entire executive board, his fingers flying across the keyboard with a frantic energy. The words flowed out of him, sharp and accusatory. He had to make them understand. If he could just find the right combination of phrases, they would see the chasm he saw, gaping behind that tiny, insignificant number.
