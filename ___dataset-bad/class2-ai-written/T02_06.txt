Robert laughed at his father-in-law's joke, a deep, hearty sound that felt utterly foreign in his own ears. For three weeks, since the termination letter, he had been playing this part: the successful, untroubled son-in-law. He felt the lie like a physical weight, a second skin he couldn't shed even for a moment. Across the polished mahogany table, his wife Claire offered a supportive smile, a gesture meant only for him, but her knuckles were white where she gripped her fork. She was his co-conspirator, propping up the fragile reality they had built for her parents. She knew the performance was costing him, saw the exhaustion behind his bright eyes that no one else seemed to notice. But her sister, Susan, was watching them both. She saw how Robert’s laughter never quite crinkled the corners of his eyes and how Claire’s posture was just a little too rigid, sensing the quiet, desperate hum of a story that wasn’t being told.
