My uncle Theodore held court in his customary fashion, his boisterous laughter echoing somewhat unnaturally against the drawing-room’s oppressive oak panelling. His anecdotes, though oft-repeated, were met with dutiful amusement, and by none more so than my cousin Eleanor, whose countenance was a mask of placid adoration. Yet, to a keen observer—and I flatter myself that I am one—the entire performance was a most transparent sham. One needed only to note the minute, persistent tremor in the hand that held his sherry glass, or the rigid set of the muscles about Eleanor’s jaw which quite belied the serene curve of her lips. No, this was not the easy bonhomie of a prosperous and united family; it was, rather, the desperate, brittle gaiety of those who stand upon the very precipice of some unspoken disaster.
