Even here, where the tranquil expanse of the downs ought to have soothed any troubled spirit, I saw the old affliction take root in my companion. It was not a sudden passion, but an insidious poison, administered drop by drop with every fresh account of his neighbour’s good fortune. A singular bitterness began to curdle his otherwise placid disposition; his own perfectly respectable holding became, in the distorted lens of his mind, a wretched and forgotten plot of land. Every triumph from the next estate—a prize-winning shire horse, a favourable marriage for a daughter—was no longer a simple fact but a calculated and personal affront. He began to see slights where none existed and to hear mockery in the rustle of the wind through the other man’s prosperous fields, for his soul had become a dark mirror, capable only of reflecting what he lacked.
