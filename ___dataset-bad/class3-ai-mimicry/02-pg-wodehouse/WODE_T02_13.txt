It is a peculiar and not altogether unpleasant sensation, I find, to be someone else entirely, particularly when that someone is a frightfully distinguished botanist from the shires, and you are navigating the swirling human currents of Piccadilly. The whole enterprise was going down like a sherry and bitters before dinner until, with the suddenness of a dropped teacup, a spanner of the most colossal proportions was bunged into the works. For there, steaming towards me like a vengeful omnibus, was the actual, bona fide Professor Pinkerton himself—a fellow of considerable tonnage with a bristling white beard that screamed authority and a deep-seated knowledge of herbaceous perennials. I did the only thing a chap in my delicate position could do. I pivoted on a sixpence, upset a costermonger’s barrow of what looked like perfectly good apples, and plunged into the thickest part of the throng, my carefully constructed rural accent melting away like a snowflake on a hot plate. One simply cannot discuss the finer points of delphiniums when the genuine article is bearing down on you, looking as if he’s about to conduct a rather stern bit of pruning.
