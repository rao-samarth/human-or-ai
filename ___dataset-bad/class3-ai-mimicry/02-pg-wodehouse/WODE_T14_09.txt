It was at that somnolent post-pudding hour, when the air is thick with cigar smoke and digestion is at its most strenuous, that a certain brand of profound thought tends to bubble to the surface of the family mind. Uncle Reginald, having surveyed the wreckage of the trifle with a glazed and benevolent eye, was suddenly moved to share just such a nugget. He informed the room at large that we are but corks bobbing on the great cosmic tide, nudged hither and thither by forces entirely beyond our ken. A fine sentiment, no doubt, but one which was quite lost on young Bertram, his nephew, who was at that very moment calculating the precise trajectory required to shin down the drainpipe outside the library window. This particular manoeuvre, while certainly involving a degree of thithering, seemed to him to be powered less by some unseen hand and more by a desperate desire to avoid Aunt Agatha’s impending slide-show of her trip to Bognor Regis. One man’s inescapable current, it appeared, was another’s pressing engagement with a bit of plumbing.
