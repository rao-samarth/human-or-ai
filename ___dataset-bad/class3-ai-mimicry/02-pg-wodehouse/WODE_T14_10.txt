Reginald sat staring into the dying embers of the fire, a position he often adopted when the slings and arrows of outrageous fortune had been doing a bit of particularly outrageous slinging. He was trying to pinpoint the precise moment at which his carefully orchestrated evening had curdled like last week's milk. Was it his decision to wear the spats, or was it the ill-advised anecdote about the walrus? It was a sticky one. A chap likes to think he's the master of his own soul, the chap-in-charge who steers the ship; but then, just as you're congratulating yourself on your superb seamanship, a vast and unforeseen wave—often shaped remarkably like one's Aunt Agatha—comes along and smacks you squarely amidships. It gives a fellow pause, it really does. It makes you wonder if you aren’t, after all, merely a passenger on a cosmic omnibus, with a driver who has a regrettable sense of humour and a shaky grasp of the route.
