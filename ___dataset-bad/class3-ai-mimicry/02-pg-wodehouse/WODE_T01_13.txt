The train rattled on, its rhythm a monotonous accompaniment to a rather bleak train of thought that had settled in somewhere around the third-class carriage. He had, of course, secured the directorship. A round of applause, a slap on the back from a fellow he’d never much cared for, and the thing was done—a neat and tidy triumph, if you squinted at it in a certain light. The trouble was, he couldn't stop thinking about poor old Weatherby, a harmless sort of cove with an unfortunate passion for collecting porcelain frogs, now presumably collecting his wits somewhere on the sidelines. There had been a moment, just a fleeting one, during that final board meeting when he had seen the look on the old fellow’s face. It wasn't anger, not precisely; it was a sort of baffled hurt, the look of a man who has been tripped up in a three-legged race by his own partner. And for what? For this rattling journey towards a new office and the quiet, beastly feeling of having played a thoroughly rotten game.
