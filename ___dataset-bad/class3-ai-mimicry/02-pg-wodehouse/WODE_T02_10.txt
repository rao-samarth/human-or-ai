“I must say, Pilkington, your cousin seems a frightfully well-travelled sort of cove,” Penelope whispered to me behind her fan, as the man in question, a specimen with a moustache that seemed to be striving for greatness against impossible odds, regaled the duchess with tales of his tea plantation in Ceylon. “He was just explaining the intricacies of high finance in Singapore.” I was about to agree when Pilkington gave a short, barking laugh that nearly sent a salted almond down the wrong pipe. “Reggie? Good heavens, no. That’s not Reggie. That’s Blenkinsop, the man who’s come to tune the piano. Aunt Agatha must have mistaken him for a guest again. I believe the farthest he’s ever travelled is to Brighton for a long weekend, and his chief financial intricacy is trying to make his weekly wage last until Thursday.” The news, delivered with such casual cheer, seemed to hang in the air for a moment before dropping like a badly made soufflé, leaving a rather sticky silence around the grand piano.
