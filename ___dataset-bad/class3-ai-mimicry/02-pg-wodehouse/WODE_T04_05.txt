It was as the train shuddered its way past Upper Crudwell that Alistair, who had been looking a bit green about the gills ever since we left the station, finally gave voice to the appalling sentiment that had evidently been bubbling within his bosom. He clutched his ticketless waistcoat, his eyes wide and pleading. “I say, Binky, old man,” he breathed, gesturing with a trembling thumb towards the approaching inspector, a beefy specimen with the unmistakable air of a man who enjoys his work far too much. “When he gets here… you couldn’t possibly feign a sudden, debilitating fit, could you? A bit of frothing, a few moans—anything to draw the fire.” I gazed at him, my jaw having sagged a trifle. This was the very chap I’d once helped winch out of a decorative pond at midnight. For him now to suggest I make a public spectacle of myself simply to save his own bacon—well, it cut a fellow to the quick, it truly did.
