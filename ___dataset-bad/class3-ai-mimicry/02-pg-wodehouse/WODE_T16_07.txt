It’s a peculiar business, the way a chap’s interior decorating can suddenly rearrange itself from a pleasant, chintz-like calm to the sort of stark, institutional panic you’d associate with a trip to the dentist. Young Reginald had been observing his host, Sir Bertram Pounce, inflate his chest preparatory to launching into his infamous after-dinner speech—a perilous monologue known to fell strong men at twenty paces—and felt his own spine perform a rather convincing impersonation of a melting icicle. To remain seated was to accept one’s doom with the quiet dignity of a doomed thing, but to rise and, say, fake a sudden coughing fit of sufficient violence to derail the proceedings required a brand of nerve he generally only read about in books. Yet, as Sir Bertram drew that first, ghastly, lung-filling breath, something new flickered in Reginald’s soul. It was a spark, a what-ho, a sudden realisation that the preservation of the sanity of a dozen innocent souls was, when you got down to brass tacks, a rather noble calling. With a lurch that sent a nearby tray of coffee cups into a deafening death-rattle, he shot to his feet, a man transformed.
