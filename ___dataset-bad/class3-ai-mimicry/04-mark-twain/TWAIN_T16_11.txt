It was the sort of suffocating Sunday dinner where every soul in the room is secretly praying for a minor catastrophe, just to liven the proceedings, and Providence, in its infinite wisdom, delivered one in the form of a wasp the size of a pistol ball. A great ruckus commenced immediately. My cousin Silas, a man who fancies himself a rugged frontiersman on account of once getting lost in his own cornfield, overturned a pitcher of buttermilk in his haste to occupy the space under my chair. The women produced a chorus of shrieks that rattled the good china. Now, a man of lesser composure might have joined the flailing and the general conflab, but I saw the situation for what it was: a simple contest of will. I snatched up Aunt Millie’s formidable hymnal—a book with enough heft to stun a mule—and with a flick of the wrist that was more instinct than calculation, I dispatched the winged marauder against the wallpaper.
