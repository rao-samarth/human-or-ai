He laid the deed upon the flat rock between us, its official-looking script a downright insult to the honest sunshine filtering through the willow branches. “It’s a providential inconvenience, is all—a clerical oversight that the good Lord saw fit to put in our path,” he said, and he had that peculiar shine in his eye, the kind a man gets when he’s finally figured out how to swindle another fellow out of the very air he breathes. I looked from his righteous face to the document, which promised a fat and comfortable future built right on the bedrock of another family’s complete and utter ruin. A fat bumblebee, a creature of considerable industry and no moral confusion whatsoever, drifted past my ear, a sight more Christian than the proposition sitting there. I reckon the Lord’s got better things to do than help you pick a widow’s pocket, and that paper’s got the Devil’s own signature on it already; you can just tell him I ain’t interested in being a co-signer.
