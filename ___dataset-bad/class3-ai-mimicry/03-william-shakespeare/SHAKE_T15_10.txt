The house held its breath, yet one sound persisted: a lone drop of water, striking the basin with the fell constancy of a hangman's drum. At first, 'twas but a small annoyance, a trifle to be mended on the morrow; but now, in the moon's pale and sickly light, it hath become a living torment, a voice that speaks without a tongue. He rose from his sweat-dampened sheets, a ghost in his own chambers, to hunt this phantom noise. Wherefore comes it? From the scullery? Nay, it echoed from the very marrow of the house, a pulse of liquid malice meant only for his ear. He felt the steady beat not in the room, but in his own blood, a rhythm driving out all thought save one. With frantic hands he began to smite the leaden pipes and tear at the wainscoting, for he knew, with a certainty that owned no reason, that to silence the drop was to silence the fiend that whispered his name from within its watery heart.
