For shame, boy,” his uncle’s voice cut through the hearth-smoke, a blade in the soft air. “You stood a statue, your blood turn’d to whey, when the brute slipp’d its tether and charged the smallest child.” The young man stared into the embers, his face a mask of memory. “My heart, sir, did knock against my ribs a fearful drum, and all my limbs were seized with a sudden ague, as if winter’s frost had claimed them in that single moment.” The old man scoffed, a rattling sound of disdain. “Valor knows no such chill.” “Nay, uncle,” he replied, his voice low yet firm, at last meeting the elder’s gaze. “For what man can boast he feels naught when the precipice doth yawn before him? A man who knows no dread is but a beast or a fool. The true reckoning lies in the hand that, though it tremble, yet performs its office; ’tis this alone that separates the hero from the wretch who flees.
