But what if this venture fails?” the younger man whispered, his face a pale moon in the candlelight. “My very blood runs cold, my lord, and a hundred phantom daggers prick my conscience.” The old knight laid a heavy hand upon his shoulder, its grip a strange comfort. “And dost thou think mine own veins are not acquainted with that self-same frost? Look you, boy; that tremor is no mark of shame, but the soul’s honest reckoning with what may come. The craven is he who lets this ague master him, who turns his back upon the appointed hour and calls his weakness wisdom. But the man of worth—he feels the chill, aye, he knows it well, and yet he makes of it a whetstone for his will. The bell shall not wait for our courage to be perfected; it tolls for what we must be.
